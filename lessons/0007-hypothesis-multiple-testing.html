Hypothesis Testing & the Multiple-Testing Trap

Lesson 007 · Foundations · ~60 min

Warm up

Lesson 006 taught you to attach an honest error bar to a single estimate. This lesson turns that error bar into a decision — "is this edge real, or is it noise?" — and then delivers the most important warning in the whole mission: the machinery that answers that question for one strategy quietly breaks the moment you ask it about the best of many. A quant researcher who does not internalize this will, with near certainty, one day present a beautiful backtest that is pure luck, and a skeptical PM will take it apart in thirty seconds. The point of today is to never be that researcher.

This lesson's single skill: read a t-statistic and a p-value correctly, and then correct them for how many strategies you actually tried. The payoff is the unit's lab — you will run a false-discovery simulation: generate hundreds of strategies with zero true edge, watch a predictable fraction of them cross the classic "significant" bar by pure chance, and see two corrections (Bonferroni and Benjamini-Hochberg) put the false discoveries back in their box. That simulation is the seed of everything Year 2 does on backtest overfitting and the deflated Sharpe ratio.

The hypothesis-testing frame

A hypothesis test is a formal procedure for deciding whether data are consistent with a default "nothing is happening" claim. It has four moving parts, and every symbol matters:

  • The null hypothesis H₀ ("H-naught") — the boring default we try to disprove. For a trading strategy it is almost always "the true mean return is zero" (μ = 0): no edge, the strategy is a coin flip after costs.
  • The alternative hypothesis H₁ — what we suspect instead (μ ≠ 0, or one-sided μ > 0: there is a real edge).
  • A test statistic — one number computed from the data that measures how far the data sit from H₀, scaled by their own noise. Below this is the t-statistic.
  • A decision rule — reject H₀ (declare a discovery) if the statistic is more extreme than a chosen threshold; otherwise fail to reject.

The logic is deliberately asymmetric, and this trips people up, so hold onto it: we never "prove H₀ true." We either gather enough evidence to reject it, or we fail to reject it — which only means "not enough evidence," not "confirmed no edge." A court analogy is exact: H₀ is "innocent," the data are the evidence, and "fail to reject" is "not guilty," never "proven innocent." We are always trying to build a case against the null.

The t-statistic — and why it is the Sharpe ratio in disguise

The workhorse test statistic for "is the mean nonzero?" is the t-statistic: the estimate divided by its own standard error (the standard error, SE, is the standard deviation of the estimate from Lesson 006).

t = θ̂ / SE(θ̂)   ⟹   for a mean return: t = μ̂ / (σ̂ / √n) = (μ̂ / σ̂) · √n

Read it as a signal-to-noise ratio: the numerator μ̂ is the estimated average return (the signal), the denominator σ̂/√n is how much that average would wobble from sample to sample (the noise), and n is the number of periods. A big t means "the estimate is many standard errors away from zero, so zero is an implausible truth."

Now the punchline that ties this whole quarter together. The quantity μ̂/σ̂ — mean over standard deviation — is the (per-period) Sharpe ratio from Lesson 006. So:

t = SRperiod · √n = SRannual · √(years)

The second form follows because annualizing multiplies the per-period Sharpe by √P (P = periods per year) while n = P · years, and the √P cancels. This tiny identity is one of the most clarifying facts in applied quant research: a strategy's t-statistic is just its annualized Sharpe ratio scaled by the square root of how many years you ran it.

Worked example (why three years is not enough). A strategy shows an annualized Sharpe of 1.0 — genuinely good-looking. Over 3 years, its t-statistic is 1.0 · √3 ≈ 1.73, which is below the classic 1.96 bar: you cannot even reject "no edge" at 5%. You would need √years ≥ 1.96, i.e. about 4 years, to clear the single-test hurdle, and (as we will see) far more if you searched for that strategy among many. This is the √n wall of Lesson 006 wearing a different hat, and it is why "I have a Sharpe of 1" is a beginning of a conversation, not the end.

The p-value: what it is, and what it is not

A threshold like 1.96 is a blunt yes/no. The p-value reports how extreme the evidence is, on a continuous 0-to-1 scale. Its definition is precise and worth memorizing verbatim:

p = P( a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed | H₀ is true )

In words: if there were truly no edge, how often would luck alone hand you a result this striking? A small p-value (say 0.01) means "data this extreme would be rare under pure noise, so noise is a strained explanation." A large p-value (0.40) means "noise explains this comfortably." The threshold you compare against is the significance level α (alpha), chosen before looking; α = 0.05 is the (arbitrary, conventional) default, and it corresponds to the two-sided |t| > 1.96 fence. Drag the observed statistic below and watch the shaded tail area — that area is the p-value:

The bell curve is the distribution of the t-statistic assuming H₀ (no edge) is true. The gold dashed fences at ±1.96 enclose the middle 95%; the light band beyond them is the 5% rejection region. The solid teal lines are the observed |t|; the dark shaded tails beyond them are the two-sided p-value. Slide right: as |t| pushes past 1.96 the tails shrink under 0.05 and the result becomes "significant."

Now the misreading that ends interviews. The p-value is P(data | H₀) — the probability of the data given the null. It is not P(H₀ | data), the probability the null is true given your data, and it is not the probability your strategy is worthless. Those are different conditional probabilities (Bayes' rule relates them, and the flip needs a prior you haven't supplied). "p = 0.05" does not mean "95% chance the edge is real." It means: "if the edge were fake, I'd see something this good 5% of the time anyway." Keep the direction of the conditioning straight and half the field's mistakes disappear.

Two ways to be wrong: Type I, Type II, and power

Because the decision is made under uncertainty, there are exactly two ways to blow it, and quant research lives and dies on which one you fear more:

H₀ true (no edge)H₀ false (real edge)
Reject H₀ (trade it)Type I error — false discoveryCorrect (true discovery)
Fail to reject (pass)CorrectType II error — missed edge

A Type I error is declaring an edge that isn't there; its probability is exactly the significance level α. A Type II error is missing a real edge; its probability is written β (beta). The power of a test is 1 − β — the chance of catching a real edge when one exists. For an allocator of capital, a Type I error is usually the expensive one: funding a strategy that is pure luck burns money and credibility, whereas passing on a marginal real edge merely leaves some money on the table. That asymmetry is why the discipline leans so hard on controlling false discoveries — which is exactly where classical testing falls apart.

The multiple-testing trap: why finance breaks classical testing

Everything above is correct for a single, pre-specified test. Quant research is almost never a single test. You try dozens of features, hundreds of parameter settings, many universes and holding periods, and you report the best. That search is where the wheels come off, and it has a precise name: the multiple-testing problem (a.k.a. data snooping, or selection bias).

Start from one clean fact. Under the null, a p-value is uniformly distributed on [0, 1]. That is what "5% significance level" means: if there is no edge, there is a 5% chance the p-value lands below 0.05 by luck. Harmless once. But run the same worthless test on M independent strategies and the false positives pile up. The field below is M strategies that all have zero true edge; every red cell is a strategy that crossed the 5% bar by chance alone:

Each square is one worthless strategy (no edge exists). Red = "significant" at the chosen threshold — a false discovery, every one. At the nominal 5% level about 5% of the field lights up no matter how often you re-run. Raise M and the count of false winners rises with it. Toggle Bonferroni to tighten the bar to α/M and watch them nearly all vanish.

The quantity that should scare you is the family-wise error rate (FWER): the probability of making at least one false discovery across the whole family of tests. For M independent true nulls each tested at level α:

FWER = 1 − (1 − α)M

Worked example. With α = 0.05 and M = 100 strategies, FWER = 1 − 0.95100 ≈ 1 − 0.006 = 0.994. So if you backtest 100 worthless ideas and report any that clear p < 0.05, you are 99.4% likely to walk away with a "significant" strategy that is pure noise. It does not even take 100: at M = 14 the FWER already passes 50%. The classic single-test bar, applied to a search, is not a filter — it is a false-discovery generator.

Correction I — Bonferroni and family-wise control

The oldest fix is the Bonferroni correction: to hold the family-wise error rate at or below α across M tests, require each individual test to clear the much stricter level α/M.

reject test i only if   pi < α / M

It works by a one-line probability bound (Boole's inequality): the chance of any of M events is at most the sum of their individual chances, so M · (α/M) = α caps the total. With M = 100 and α = 0.05, each strategy must now clear p < 0.0005 — which for a two-sided test means roughly |t| > 3.48, not 1.96. That is the "Bonferroni on" state in the widget: the false discoveries almost entirely disappear.

The cost of Bonferroni: it is brutally conservative. Two problems. First, it controls the probability of even one false positive, which is often stricter than you need — in research you can tolerate a few false leads if most discoveries are real. Second, it assumes the tests are (nearly) independent; real strategies are highly correlated (a hundred momentum variants are almost the same bet), so the effective number of independent tests is smaller than M and Bonferroni over-corrects, killing power — you throw away real edges to avoid any false one. This tension motivates the second, more modern correction.

Correction II — the false discovery rate (Benjamini-Hochberg)

Instead of "never make even one false discovery," control the false discovery rate (FDR): the expected proportion of your discoveries that are false. If you reject R hypotheses and V of them are truly null, FDR = E[V / R]. Targeting, say, an FDR of 10% says "I'm content if about one in ten of my 'winners' is a fluke" — a far more sensible bargain for a research pipeline than Bonferroni's zero-tolerance stance, and it keeps much more power.

The Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) procedure delivers FDR control with a simple step-up rule:

  1. Sort the M p-values ascending: p₍₁₎ ≤ p₍₂₎ ≤ … ≤ p₍M₎.
  2. Find the largest rank k for which p₍k₎ ≤ (k / M) · q, where q is your target FDR (e.g. 0.10).
  3. Reject (declare real) all hypotheses with rank 1 through k.

Worked example. Say M = 10 strategies and q = 0.10. Sorted p-values are 0.001, 0.008, 0.02, 0.04, 0.13, …. Compare each to (k/10)·0.10, i.e. the thresholds 0.010, 0.020, 0.030, 0.040, 0.050, …: rank 1 (0.001 ≤ 0.010) ✓, rank 2 (0.008 ≤ 0.020) ✓, rank 3 (0.02 ≤ 0.030) ✓, rank 4 (0.04 ≤ 0.040) ✓, rank 5 (0.13 ≤ 0.050) ✗. The largest passing rank is k = 4, so BH declares the first four significant — even though a pure Bonferroni bar (p < 0.005) would have kept only the first. BH recovered two real-looking discoveries Bonferroni would have discarded, at the controlled cost of an expected ~10% false-discovery share.

Dependence: Benjamini-Yekutieli (BHY). Plain BH assumes independence or a mild positive dependence. Financial tests are strongly and messily dependent, so the Benjamini-Yekutieli (BHY) variant divides the threshold by c(M) = Σi=1..M 1/i ≈ ln M + 0.577, buying validity under arbitrary dependence at the price of some power. Harvey, Liu & Zhu build their finance-specific recommendations on exactly this machinery.

The Sharpe / t-stat haircut and Harvey-Liu-Zhu

Put the pieces together and you get the single most-cited practical rule of this literature. Because a t-statistic is SRannual·√years, correcting the significance bar upward for multiple testing is identical to haircutting a reported Sharpe ratio: the more strategies you searched, the higher the |t| a survivor must clear, and equivalently the more you must shave off its headline Sharpe before believing it. The curve below shows how far above the naive 1.96 the bar climbs as the number of tested strategies grows:

The required |t| to stay at an overall 5% false-positive rate (Bonferroni), against the number of strategies tested (log axis). At M = 1 it is the familiar 1.96; by M = 100 it is ≈ 3.5, above Harvey-Liu-Zhu's rule-of-thumb ≈ 3.0 line. Slide it: a backtest with t = 2.5 that looks like a clear winner alone is not significant once you admit you tried 100 ideas.

Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016) — the primary reading — surveyed decades of published equity "factors" and argued that, given how much the profession has collectively data-mined, the conventional |t| > 1.96 bar is far too lenient: most published factors would not survive an honest multiple-testing correction. Their headline recommendation is that a genuinely new factor should clear roughly |t| > 3.0, and they provide explicit "haircuts" that translate a reported Sharpe into a deflated, multiple-testing-aware one. This is the academic mirror of the deflated Sharpe ratio (Bailey & López de Prado) you meet in Year 2 (unit 055), which does the same deflation at the level of a single researcher's own backtest search.

Explain it back

Trap — the best of many backtests. The failure mode this entire lesson exists to prevent: you (or your research group, or the whole published literature) try many strategies and report the one with the best t-stat, at the nominal 5% bar. That "winner" is drawn from the maximum of many noisy statistics, and the maximum of M pure-noise t-stats drifts upward like √(2 ln M) — so it looks significant precisely because you selected it. Three concrete forms: (1) data snooping — grid-searching parameters and reporting the peak; (2) p-hacking — tweaking the universe, dates, or filters until p dips under 0.05; (3) publication / survivorship bias — you only see the strategies that worked, never the thousands that were quietly binned. The defenses are everything above (Bonferroni / BH / BHY on the true number of trials) plus, at the strategy level, the deflated Sharpe ratio and the leakage-free cross- validation (CPCV) of Year 2. Always report how many things you tried. A t-stat without a trial count is not evidence.

Common questions (and honest answers)

“Doesn't p = 0.05 mean there's a 95% chance my strategy is real?”
No — that swaps the conditioning. The p-value is P(data this extreme | no edge), not P(edge is real | data). Getting from one to the other requires Bayes' rule and a prior probability that the edge is real, which the p-value alone never supplies. In a field where the base rate of real edges is low and search is heavy, a nominally "significant" result can still be far more likely noise than signal.
“If Bonferroni controls the error, why bother with FDR?”
Bonferroni controls the probability of any false positive (FWER), which is stricter than most research needs and destroys power — especially with correlated tests, where it badly over-corrects. FDR controls the expected fraction of your discoveries that are false, a more useful target for a pipeline that can tolerate a few false leads to keep the real ones. Different guarantees for different jobs.
“How many tests should I count as M — only the ones I ran?”
Count every idea you effectively tried, including the ones you abandoned early and the variations your intuition pre-filtered — and, at the field level, the ones others published before you. This is genuinely hard to pin down, which is the honest, uncomfortable core of the problem. When in doubt, assume you tried more than you remember; the deflated Sharpe ratio (Year 2) formalizes estimating the effective number of trials.
“My 200 strategies are all correlated — isn't Bonferroni with M = 200 wrong?”
It is too conservative, yes. Correlation means your effective number of independent tests is well below 200, so dividing by 200 over-corrects and kills real edges. That's exactly why BHY (valid under arbitrary dependence) and simulation-based nulls that preserve the correlation structure are preferred in finance over vanilla Bonferroni.
“Is a low p-value on a long backtest enough to trade?”
No. Statistical significance is necessary, not sufficient. You still need economic significance (does the edge survive costs and capacity?), out-of-sample and leakage-free validation, and a correction for how many strategies produced this one. A t = 2 from a search of hundreds is not evidence of anything.

The Quant Landscape

Lesson 001 · Foundations · ~35 min

You're aiming for a Quantitative Researcher seat at a top systematic firm. Before any math, you need the map: who does what, on which side of the market, at what speed — and where your job sits. Getting this wrong is how people spend a year studying the wrong things (e.g. grinding kernel-bypass networking for a role that never touches it).

This lesson's single skill: place any quant role, firm, and strategy on three axes — buy-side vs sell-side, the QR/QT/QD split, and trading frequency — and state what "alpha" means.

Axis 1 — Buy-side vs sell-side

SideWhat they doExamples
Sell-sideBanks/dealers: make markets, price & hedge derivatives, provide liquidity to clientsGoldman, Morgan Stanley, JPM
Buy-sideTake positions with their own/investors' capital to make money from returnsTwo Sigma, DE Shaw, Citadel, HRT, Jane Street

Your target is the buy-side, systematic world: firms that deploy capital based on models and code, not a portfolio manager's gut. This is where the QR craft — signals, validation, backtesting — lives.

Axis 2 — QR / QT / QD

RoleOwnsDaily work
QR — Quant Researcher (you)The signal: what to trade and whyData, features, models, and above all validating that an edge is real
QT — Quant TraderPricing, risk, execution decisionsFast reasoning, market-making, managing live risk
QD — Quant DeveloperThe systems: data pipelines, backtesters, low-latency infraC++/Rust, performance, reliability

The boundaries blur (at Jane Street or HRT a "researcher" writes a lot of production code), but the center of gravity differs. Our curriculum is QR-centric: you'll learn enough QT (pricing, microstructure) and QD (systems, latency) to collaborate and interview, not to switch tracks.

Axis 3 — Frequency

Speed dictates almost everything — data, models, infra, and the source of edge. 

HFT (µs–ms)        market making, latency arbitrage      QD-heavy, tiny per-trade edge
Mid-freq (min–days) statistical arbitrage, ML signals     QR sweet spot
Low-freq (weeks+)   factor investing, risk premia          research-heavy, capacity-large

As a QR targeting systematic firms, you'll mostly live in mid-frequency: horizons where machine learning on market and alternative data can find structure, and where careful validation — not raw speed — decides who wins.

What is "alpha"?

Alpha is return not explained by exposure to known risk factors — the part of performance attributable to genuine skill/edge rather than simply being paid for bearing market risk. Formally, in r = α + β·r_market + ε, alpha is the intercept α: what you earn independent of the market's move. A QR's job is to find, validate, and defend sources of α.

Where the real difficulty is

The core trap of this whole field. Markets are adversarial and low signal-to-noise. If you torture historical data enough, you will always find a beautiful "strategy" — that is pure overfitting, and it will lose money live. The single most valuable QR skill is not a fancier model; it is the discipline to prove you did not fool yourself (leakage-free pipelines, purged cross-validation, backtest-overfitting tests). Keep this in mind from day one — the entirety of Year 2 Q2 exists to make you rigorous here.

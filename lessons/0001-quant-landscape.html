The Quant Landscape

Lesson 001 · Foundations · ~55 min

You're aiming for a Quantitative Researcher seat at a top systematic firm — Two Sigma, DE Shaw, Cubist, Citadel/GQS, Jane Street QR, HRT, and peers. Before any math, you need the map: who does what, on which side of the market, at what speed — and where your job sits. This is not throat-clearing. Getting the map wrong is how capable people spend a year studying the wrong things: grinding kernel-bypass networking for a role that never touches it, or memorizing option Greeks for a mid-frequency equity seat that lives and dies on data hygiene.

The whole field is unusually adversarial. Unlike most of engineering — where a correct program stays correct — a trading edge decays the moment enough people find it, and the data you learn from is mostly noise. So the map you build today has a second purpose: it tells you which skills compound (validation, statistics, clean pipelines) versus which are locally useful but narrow (a specific venue's order-entry protocol). We optimize relentlessly for the first kind.

This lesson's single skill: place any quant role, firm, and strategy on three axes — buy-side vs sell-side, the QR/QT/QD split, and trading frequency — and state precisely what "alpha" means. By the end you should be able to hear a firm described in a news article and locate it on all three axes in one sentence, and sketch the research lifecycle this entire course is organized around.

Axis 1 — Buy-side vs sell-side

The first cut divides the industry by whose money is at risk and why.

SideWhat they doExamples
Sell-sideBanks/dealers: make markets, price & hedge derivatives, provide liquidity and financing to clients. They earn the spread and fees, not the direction.Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPM
Buy-sideFunds: take positions with their own and investors' capital to profit from returns. They earn (or lose) the direction and the edge.Two Sigma, DE Shaw, Citadel, HRT, Jane Street

The names are historical: the sell-side sells products and services (underwriting, market-making, structured derivatives) to the buy-side, which buys and holds risk to generate returns. A dealer ideally wants to be flat at the end of the day — paid the spread for facilitating flow, not exposed to where the market goes. A fund wants to be positioned — it is trying to be exposed, but only to risks it believes it is being over-compensated for.

Your target is the buy-side, systematic world: firms that deploy capital based on models and code, not a portfolio manager's gut. This is where the QR craft — signals, validation, backtesting — lives, and where a strong programmer with disciplined statistics has the clearest edge. One nuance worth holding: the two sides need each other. A systematic fund's orders are often filled by a sell-side or electronic market maker; the fund is that market maker's "flow." Understanding both sides of a single trade — who provides liquidity and who demands it — is a recurring theme once we reach microstructure.

Axis 2 — QR / QT / QD

Within a systematic firm, three quant roles divide the work. The boundaries blur, but the center of gravity of each is distinct.

RoleOwnsDaily work
QR — Quant Researcher (you)The signal: what to trade and whyData, features, models, and above all validating that an edge is real
QT — Quant TraderPricing, risk, and execution decisionsFast reasoning, market-making, managing live risk in real time
QD — Quant DeveloperThe systems: data pipelines, backtesters, low-latency infraC++/Rust, performance, correctness, reliability under load

A QR's week looks like: pull a dataset, form a hypothesis ("order-flow imbalance predicts short-horizon returns in liquid names"), engineer features that respect the arrow of time, fit a model, and then spend most of the effort trying to break the result — checking for leakage, overfitting, and whether the "edge" survives realistic costs. The output is a signal specification and a defensible backtest, handed to QTs and QDs to trade and productionize.

The lines genuinely blur by firm. At Jane Street or HRT a "researcher" writes a great deal of production code; at a large multi-manager platform the roles are more siloed. There is also a fourth title you'll see — Quant Strategist / "quant" on the sell-side — which is a different job again (pricing and risk libraries for a bank's desks). Our curriculum is QR-centric: you will learn enough QT (pricing, microstructure) and QD (systems, latency) to collaborate and interview credibly — not to switch tracks.

Axis 3 — Frequency

Speed dictates almost everything downstream: what data you need, what models are feasible, how much infra matters, and — crucially — where the edge comes from. Commit a prediction before you read the table: 

HFT (µs–ms)         market making, latency arbitrage      QD-heavy, tiny per-trade edge × huge volume
Mid-freq (min–days) statistical arbitrage, ML signals      QR sweet spot: structure in data
Low-freq (weeks+)   factor investing, risk premia          research-heavy, large capacity

At high frequency, the per-trade edge is minuscule — a fraction of a tick — but it is harvested millions of times, so the game is won on latency and queue position (a QD/infra game). At the mid-frequency horizon, ML on market and alternative data can find genuine structure, and the winner is decided by model and validation quality, not nanoseconds — this is the QR sweet spot and where this course lives. At low frequency, you are largely being paid for bearing well-understood risk premia; capacity is huge but raw edge per dollar is thin.

Frequency also decides how much data you effectively have. A strategy that trades once a month over 10 years has ~120 independent bets — a tiny sample to prove skill on. A strategy trading thousands of times a day accumulates statistical evidence far faster. This "how many independent bets do I actually have?" question silently governs how confident you're allowed to be, and it will resurface every time we discuss significance and overfitting.

Archetypes of a systematic firm

"Systematic buy-side" is not one business model. Three archetypes recur, and knowing which one a firm is tells you what a QR's day and incentives look like there.

ArchetypeHow it's organizedFlavor examples
Multi-manager "pod" platformMany small teams (pods) each run their own book with tight risk limits; the platform allocates capital and cuts underperformers fastCitadel, Millennium, Point72/Cubist
Single-strategy / centralizedOne firm-wide research effort feeds a shared alpha pool; researchers contribute signals to a common combineRenaissance (Medallion), Two Sigma, DE Shaw
Market-maker → fundGrew from electronic market-making; deep infra/latency DNA, increasingly running systematic alpha tooHRT, Jane Street, Jump, Tower

The organizational shape matters to you. On a pod platform, a signal must pass your pod's risk and a short leash on drawdown; individual accountability is high and so is turnover. In a centralized shop, your signal is one input into a giant portfolio and the bar is "does it add orthogonal, uncorrelated alpha to what we already have?" — which makes correlation to existing signals as important as raw performance. At a market-maker-turned-fund, you'll rub shoulders with world-class infra and be expected to respect latency and execution realities from day one. None of this changes the core QR craft; it changes what "a good result" has to clear.

What is "alpha"?

Alpha is return not explained by exposure to known risk factors — the part of performance attributable to genuine skill/edge rather than simply being paid for bearing market risk. Formally, in r = α + β·r_market + ε, alpha is the intercept α: what you earn independent of the market's move.

Make it concrete. Suppose your strategy returned 12% over a year, the market returned 10%, and your strategy's market exposure (beta) was 0.5. The part of your return "explained" by simply riding the market is β·r_market = 0.5 × 10% = 5%. The remaining 12% − 5% = 7% is your alpha — the piece you'd have to defend as real skill. Anyone can manufacture return by cranking up beta (leverage); alpha is the scarce thing, and it is what a QR is hired to find, validate, and defend.

The idea generalizes beyond the market factor. Decades of research identified other systematic risk factors that pay a premium — size, value, momentum, quality, and so on. Modern alpha is measured net of all the factors your employer already harvests cheaply: if your "signal" is just momentum in disguise, it is not alpha to a firm that already runs momentum. So "is this alpha?" really means "is this return left over after regressing out every exposure we can already get for free?" That is a high bar, and much of Year 2 is about clearing it honestly.

A related word you'll meet constantly is the Sharpe ratio — roughly, annualized excess return divided by its volatility — a first-pass measure of how much edge you got per unit of risk. A single great year can be luck; a Sharpe of 2 sustained across regimes is a business. Year 2 is largely about not fooling yourself with an inflated Sharpe.

Alpha decays: crowding and capacity

Here is the property that makes this field unlike almost any other engineering discipline: a published or crowded edge stops working. When many participants trade the same signal, their orders move the price toward fair value before you can profit — the very act of harvesting alpha destroys it. Two consequences shape a QR's whole career:

  • Alpha decay. A signal's edge tends to erode over time as it's discovered and arbitraged away. Research is not a one-time conquest; it's a treadmill of finding new structure as old structure fades.
  • Capacity. Every strategy has a maximum size it can deploy before its own market impact eats the edge. A signal that makes a fortune on \$10M might lose money trying to run \$5B, because pushing that much size moves prices against you. "What's the capacity?" is one of the first questions a PM asks, and it's why a beautiful backtest on tiny size can still be worthless.

This is why the mission targets net, capacity-aware Sharpe — not a headline number. Keep the treadmill in mind: it reframes "I found an edge" into "I found an edge that is currently uncrowded and has room to scale," which is a much harder and more valuable claim.

The research lifecycle (the whole course in one arc)

Everything in this curriculum slots into a single pipeline that turns a raw idea into deployed, defended capital. It's worth seeing the whole thing now so each future lesson has a home: 

idea/hypothesis → data → features (signal) → model → VALIDATION → portfolio → execution → live P&L
     ↑                                                                                    │
     └──────────────────────  monitor, attribute, iterate (alpha decays)  ────────────────┘
  1. Idea / hypothesis. A testable claim about why an edge exists (an economic or behavioral reason beats a data-mined coincidence).
  2. Data. Prices, order books, fundamentals, alternative data — cleaned and, crucially, point-in-time (no future leaking in).
  3. Features / signal. Transformations of data that carry predictive content, engineered to respect the arrow of time.
  4. Model. Maps features to a forecast — often simpler than newcomers expect, because complex models overfit noisy data.
  5. Validation. The heart of the job: purged/embargoed cross-validation, deflated Sharpe, backtest-overfitting tests. This is where most "edges" die — as they should.
  6. Portfolio. Combine many signals into positions under risk and correlation constraints (denoised covariance, sizing).
  7. Execution. Trade the target positions while minimizing spread and market impact.
  8. Live & monitor. Track realized vs expected, attribute P&L, and iterate as edges decay.

Notice the mass of the course sits on validation. That's deliberate: steps 1–4 are shared with ordinary ML, but step 5 is where quant research is uniquely unforgiving, and it's the skill that transfers across every firm and strategy.

Why this is harder than ordinary machine learning

You're a strong programmer, so it's worth being explicit about why your ML instincts will occasionally betray you here. Four properties make financial data a hostile environment:

Extremely low signal-to-noise ratio
In image classification the signal is overwhelming; in returns, an R² of a few percent can be excellent. Most of what you see is noise, so flexible models happily fit the noise and call it signal.
Non-stationarity
The data-generating process changes — regimes shift, volatility rises and falls, relationships break. "Train on the past, test on the future" is not a nicety here; the future is genuinely a different distribution, which is why naïve i.i.d. cross-validation lies.
Adversarial feedback
Other smart, well-capitalized participants are actively competing to remove the very patterns you find. Your actions move prices; the environment reacts to you. There is no analogue to this in classifying cats.
Small effective sample
Years of daily data sound like a lot, but returns are autocorrelated in volatility and overlapping in information, so your number of truly independent observations is far smaller than the row count. Confidence intervals are wider than they look.

Every one of these pushes in the same direction: toward humility, simpler models, and ruthless validation. That's the temperament the mission is training.

Where the real difficulty is

The core trap of this whole field. Markets are adversarial and low signal-to-noise. If you torture historical data enough, you will always find a beautiful "strategy" — that is pure overfitting, and it will lose money live. The single most valuable QR skill is not a fancier model; it is the discipline to prove you did not fool yourself (leakage-free pipelines, purged cross-validation, backtest-overfitting tests). Keep this in mind from day one — the entirety of Year 2 Q2 exists to make you rigorous here.

Explain it back

Common questions (and honest answers)

“Isn't this just applied machine learning?”
ML is a tool in the kit, but the hard part is not fitting a model — it's the evaluation. In most ML, a held-out test set is trustworthy; in markets, naive test sets leak the future, regimes shift, and a great backtest routinely means nothing. The scarce skill is honest validation on non-stationary, adversarial data. That's why this track front-loads statistics and leakage, not architectures.
“Do I need a finance background to start?”
No. You're starting from near-zero finance and that's expected — Year 1 Q1 builds markets vocabulary from scratch (this lesson is step one). Strong math and programming are the durable base; the finance is learnable and we build it deliberately.
“Isn't HFT where the real money and prestige are?”
HFT is a superb business, but it's a QD/infra-dominated game won on latency, and it is out of scope here except at awareness depth. Mid-frequency systematic research rewards exactly your profile — statistics, code, and validation discipline — and is where a QR's edge compounds.
“If edges decay, isn't the whole thing hopeless?”
No — it's a treadmill, not a dead end. New data sources, new inefficiencies, and structural changes continually create fresh edges. What decays is any specific, crowded signal. The durable asset is your process for finding and validating the next one, which is exactly what we're building.

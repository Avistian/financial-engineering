Probability Refresher for Quants

Lesson 005 · Foundations · ~55 min

Warm up

You already have solid undergraduate probability; this lesson doesn't reteach it. Instead it sharpens that knowledge into the quant working set — the handful of moments, distributions, dependence tools, and one core idea (conditioning) you will reach for constantly, framed the way quants use them. It's also the on-ramp to the measure-theoretic view formalized in Lesson 011: think of moments and conditioning concretely now, filtrations and rigor later.

This lesson's single skill: pick the right distribution for a modeling job and reason with the first four moments and with dependence (covariance) — and see why "a forecast" is really a conditional expectation.

The four moments you actually use

MomentMeaningIn markets
Mean (μ)center / expected valuedrift, expected return
Variance (σ²)spread around the meanrisk / volatility (σ = std. dev.)
Skewnessasymmetry of the distributioncrash risk (equities have negative skew)
Kurtosistail heaviness / peakednessfat tails (Lesson 004)

A normal distribution has skewness 0 and kurtosis 3 (so "excess kurtosis" — kurtosis minus 3 — is 0). Real equity returns have negative skew (big down moves are larger and more frequent than big up moves) and positive excess kurtosis (fat tails). Those two departures from normality — which you measured in Lesson 004 — are exactly why the choice of distribution below is not a formality. A useful discipline: whenever you summarize returns, report at least the first two moments, and be suspicious of any claim that ignores the third and fourth.

Two relationships are worth internalizing because they recur in microstructure. First, the Bernoulli → Binomial → Normal chain: a single up/down move is Bernoulli, a sum of them is Binomial, and by the CLT many of them approach a Normal — this is literally how the binomial option-pricing tree converges to Black-Scholes. Second, the Poisson ↔ Exponential duality: if events (say, trades) arrive as a Poisson process at rate λ, the counts per interval are Poisson and the gaps between events are Exponential. When trade arrivals cluster (they do — think volatility clustering's cousin), the constant-rate Poisson fails and you reach for a Hawkes process (self-exciting arrivals), which Year 2 builds on exactly this foundation.

The lognormal deserves its own moment. If a price evolves by multiplying random factors — as returns compound — then by the same log-turns-products-into-sums logic from Lesson 004, the log price is a sum of increments. If those increments are normal, the log price is normal, so the price itself is the exponential of a normal: lognormal. That's why prices (which can't go negative) are modeled lognormally while their log returns are modeled normally — and it's the distributional backbone of Black-Scholes.

Covariance, correlation, and the covariance matrix

A single asset's risk is its variance; a portfolio's risk depends on how assets move together. That co-movement is covariance, and its normalized, unit-free version is correlation (between −1 and +1). The key formula — the one that makes diversification work — is the variance of a weighted sum:

Var(aX + bY) = a²Var(X) + b²Var(Y) + 2ab·Cov(X, Y)

That cross term is the whole game. Combine two assets each with variance σ². If they are uncorrelated, an equal-weight mix has variance (σ²/4 + σ²/4) = σ²/2 — half the risk of holding one, for the same expected return. Extend to n equally-weighted uncorrelated assets and the portfolio variance falls to σ²/n: risk shrinks with the number of independent bets. This is the mathematical statement of "diversification is the only free lunch," and it's why a QR cares less about any single signal's strength than about finding signals whose errors are uncorrelated with the ones already in the book. The full bookkeeping for many assets is the covariance matrix Σ — a symmetric matrix of every pairwise covariance — and estimating it well from noisy data (Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage, random-matrix denoising) is a whole Year-2 topic.

Independent vs uncorrelated (a costly confusion)

Zero correlation does not mean independence. Correlation measures only linear association. A classic counterexample: let X be symmetric around 0 and let Y = X². Then Cov(X, Y) = 0 — zero correlation — yet Y is completely determined by X. They are perfectly dependent and linearly uncorrelated at once. This is not a math-class curiosity: markets are full of nonlinear and tail dependence. Assets that look uncorrelated in calm times crash together in a crisis (correlations "go to 1" in tail events), so a risk model built only on linear correlation systematically understates joint disaster risk. Independence is a strong claim; uncorrelatedness is a weak one — never silently upgrade the second to the first.

Bayes' rule — updating a belief with evidence

P(A | B) = P(B | A) · P(A) / P(B)

Bayes' rule tells you how to revise a prior belief P(A) into a posterior P(A|B) after observing evidence B. It's the engine behind a huge amount of quant thinking: updating a regime probability as new data arrives, combining a prior view with noisy signals (Black-Litterman portfolio construction is Bayesian at heart), and reasoning about base rates. The base-rate lesson is the practical one: a test that's "95% accurate" for a rare event still produces mostly false positives if the event is rare enough, because P(A) is tiny. Quants who ignore base rates chase signals that are almost all noise — the same overfitting trap in probabilistic clothing.

Law of large numbers vs the central limit theorem

Two theorems anchor how much you can trust an average, and conflating them is common. The law of large numbers (LLN) says the sample mean converges to the true mean as the sample grows — it promises the average lands in the right place eventually. The central limit theorem (CLT) says the sample mean's distribution becomes approximately normal with standard deviation σ/√n — it tells you how uncertain that average still is. The √n is the punchline for a QR: to halve your error bar you need four times the data, which is why proving a small edge is real takes so much history. And both theorems carry fine print that markets violate — the CLT needs finite variance (fat tails strain it) and effectively independent observations (autocorrelation shrinks your true n below the row count). Your error bars are almost always wider than a naïve σ/√n suggests.

Conditioning: the idea everything is built on

A conditional expectation E[X | information] is your best forecast of X given what you currently know. This is not one technique among many — it is the shape of almost every model you will ever build. A regression predicts E[y | features]. A trading signal is E[future return | today's data]. An option price is (a discounted) expected payoff conditional on current market information. Internalize the plain idea now: expectation given what you currently observe.

Tower rule: E[ E[X | ℱ] ] = E[X]

The tower rule (law of iterated expectations) says: if you average your conditional forecasts over all the things you might have observed, you recover the unconditional average. A worked example makes it click. Flip a fair coin; on heads, X is drawn with mean 10, on tails with mean 2. Then E[X | heads] = 10 and E[X | tails] = 2, and averaging over the coin: E[X] = 0.5·10 + 0.5·2 = 6. Forecasting and then averaging over the information can't change the overall mean — it just reorganizes it. In Lesson 011 you'll meet the formal version: conditioning on a filtration ℱ (the information available up to time t), and the fact that E[·|ℱ] is a projection onto what's knowable — the machinery behind martingales and no-arbitrage pricing.

Trap. Reaching for the normal distribution by default. It underlies the CLT and Brownian motion, but it badly models returns (tails too thin) and prices (which can't go negative), and its correlation captures only linear dependence. Using a normal where a Student-t or lognormal belongs, or trusting zero correlation as independence, is how risk gets systematically underestimated. Match the tool to the quantity: normal for increments/errors, lognormal for prices, Student-t for fat-tailed returns, Poisson/Exponential for event timing — and remember correlation ≠ dependence.

Explain it back

Common questions (and honest answers)

“Doesn't the Central Limit Theorem justify using the normal everywhere?”
The CLT justifies the normal for sums of many independent, finite-variance pieces — a strong set of conditions. Returns violate them: they're heavy-tailed and not independent (volatility clusters), and real data is finite. The CLT is a limit, not a license to assume normality of the thing you actually observe.
“Do I need measure theory to do any of this?”
Not yet. The concrete versions — moments, distributions, covariance, conditional expectation as "best forecast given info" — carry you through Q1. Measure theory (Lesson 011) makes conditioning and filtrations rigorous so that stochastic calculus is well-defined; you'll want it before Q2, not before this lesson.
“If two signals are uncorrelated, can I treat them as independent bets?”
Only cautiously. Zero correlation rules out linear co-movement, not nonlinear or tail dependence. Two "uncorrelated" strategies can still blow up together in a crisis. Diversification math assumes more than correlation reports, so stress-test the joint tail, don't just check ρ.
“Why model prices as lognormal instead of just normal?”
A normal price could go negative, which is nonsensical for a stock, and it doesn't match how prices compound multiplicatively. The lognormal is positive and arises naturally when log returns are normal — it's the honest distribution for a quantity built by compounding.

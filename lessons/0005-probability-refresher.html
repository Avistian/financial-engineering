Probability Refresher for Quants

Lesson 005 · Foundations · ~40 min

You have solid undergrad probability; this lesson sharpens it into the quant working set and the four distributions you'll reach for constantly. It's also the on-ramp to the measure-theoretic view (Lesson 011) — think of moments and conditioning now, filtrations later.

This lesson's single skill: pick the right distribution for a modeling job and reason with the first four moments.

A normal distribution has skew 0 and kurtosis 3 ("excess kurtosis" 0). Real returns have negative skew and excess kurtosis > 0 — which is exactly why the next distribution matters.

Conditioning: the idea everything is built on

A conditional expectation E[X | information] is your best forecast of X given what you know. Almost every model — a regression, a signal, an option price — is a conditional expectation of a payoff given current information. In Lesson 011 you'll see the formal version: conditioning on a filtration (the information available up to time t) and the fact that E[·|ℱ] is a projection. Get comfortable now with the plain idea: expectation given what you currently observe.

Tower rule: E[ E[X | ℱ] ] = E[X]
Trap. Reaching for the normal distribution by default. It underlies the CLT and Brownian motion, but it badly models returns (too-thin tails) and prices (which can't go negative). Using a normal where a Student-t or lognormal belongs is how risk gets systematically underestimated. Match the distribution to the quantity: normal for increments/errors, lognormal for prices, Student-t for fat-tailed returns, Poisson for counts.

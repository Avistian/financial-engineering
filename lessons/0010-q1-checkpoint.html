Q1 Checkpoint: Statistical Hygiene Checkpoint

Lesson 010 · Exit exam for Units 001–009 · ~60 min

Warm up

This is a checkpoint, not a new topic. Nine lessons ago you started from zero: what a quant researcher actually does (001), the instruments and the order book (002–003), the shape of returns (004), the probability language (005), and then the four-lesson statistical-hygiene spine — estimation and uncertainty (006), hypothesis testing and the multiple-testing trap (007), the covariance geometry behind PCA (008), and regression with a trustworthy standard error (009). The checkpoint asks one thing: can you take a returns-analysis notebook that claims an edge and either kill it or confirm it — catching a planted spurious signal — without being told where the flaw is? That is the smallest possible version of your whole job, and per the rules of this track (SESSION.md) you do not advance to Q2 until you can pass it cold.

Everything in Q1 pointed here. The mission (MISSION.md) is not "learn some statistics"; it is to reach the level where "when you claim an edge, you know exactly what you are standing on." A checkpoint is where that claim gets tested. So this lesson does three things: it re-assembles the quarter into a single audit checklist, it teaches the one integrative skill the pieces were building toward — the spurious-signal autopsy — and it hands you the exit-ticket lab where a signal with a gaudy t-statistic is waiting to fool you.

What a checkpoint is (and why it is strict)

In this curriculum a unit is a concept plus its lab plus its quiz; a checkpoint is the exit exam for a whole quarter, and it is deliberately harder than any single lesson because it is cumulative and un-signposted. In a normal lesson you know the flaw is the one just taught — of course the overlap inflated the t-stat, the lesson is about overlap. In the real job, and in this checkpoint, nobody tells you which of the five failure modes is present, or whether several are stacked. The skill being certified is diagnosis under uncertainty: run the full battery, find what breaks, and report it honestly — including the outcome that a PM least wants to hear, "there is no edge here."

Why strict? Because the entire economic value of a quant researcher is negative knowledge: the ability to not stake capital on noise. A backtest you correctly killed is worth more than a backtest you wrongly shipped, because the shipped one loses real money out of sample while quietly destroying your credibility. The teach-skill's motto for this workspace is literal here: "a killed bad backtest is a win." Passing this checkpoint means you can be trusted to kill your own darlings.

The quarter in one diagram: five questions of statistical hygiene

Here is the whole quarter compressed into the audit you will run on any claimed edge. Each row is a question, the lesson that answers it, the failure mode if you skip it, and the test you actually run. Learn this table as a table — it is the checklist you carry into every signal study for the next three years.

QuestionFromFailure mode if skippedThe test you run
1. Is the number even measurable? Do you have enough independent data to see an effect this small? 006 You "measure" an edge smaller than your standard error — pure noise dressed as signal. Standard error (SE ∝ 1/√n); a confidence interval or bootstrap CI that must exclude zero.
2. How many things did you try? Is this the best of a search, reported as if it were the only test? 007 Multiple-testing / selection bias: with 100 zero-edge tries, a "t = 3 winner" is nearly guaranteed by chance. The fair bar is not 1.96 — it is the best-of-M null (Bonferroni / BH / the expected max). Haircut the t.
3. Are your observations really independent? Do the errors persist or overlap? 009 Autocorrelation / overlap shrinks the effective sample; the naïve t is inflated by multiples. Recompute the SE with Newey–West (HAC); check n_eff.
4. Is the error variance constant? Or do the loud days / noisy names drive the fit? 009 Heteroskedasticity mis-states the SE (usually too small); the t is overstated. White / HC robust standard errors.
5. Did the future leak in? And are you regressing stationary things? 004, 008, 009 Leakage (full-sample scaling, look-ahead) or spurious regression on non-stationary levels manufactures a huge, fake t. Strict temporal discipline; regress returns, not prices; recheck any t that survives.

Notice what every failure mode has in common — the load-bearing insight of the whole quarter, which you proved formally in Lesson 009 and should now say in your sleep: in every one of these, the point estimate can be perfectly fine; it is the standard error — and therefore the t-statistic, the claim — that is the lie. A checkpoint failure is almost never "your β̂ was wrong." It is "you believed a t-statistic you had not earned the right to believe."

The one skill: the spurious-signal autopsy

The integrative skill this checkpoint certifies is a procedure: given a claimed edge with a headline t-statistic, subject it to the five questions in order of how much they can inflate a t, and watch what survives. Call it the autopsy. It matters that you run all five even after one already kills the signal, because in the real world the flaws stack — a signal can be simultaneously overlap-inflated and cherry-picked and leakage-tainted, and a defensible write-up names all of them, not just the first.

Two of those audits produce a clean, quantitative haircut you can compute on the spot, so let us make them concrete before the visual. First, overlap (question 3): from Lesson 009, overlapping h-period returns give errors that are strongly positively autocorrelated, and for AR(1)-like persistence the honest standard error is larger than the naïve one by roughly √[(1+φ)/(1−φ)]. The honest t is the reported t divided by that inflation factor. Second, the search (question 2): from Lesson 007, if the reported t was the best of M tried strategies, the fair benchmark is not the single-test 1.96 — it is the expected maximum of M draws under the null, E[max of M]. A "surviving" statistic subtracts that bar. So the autopsy turns one gaudy headline number into a chain:

treported  →  tNW = treported / √[(1+φ)/(1−φ)]  →  tsurvives = tNW − E[max of M]

The widget below runs exactly this chain on a headline t = 6.2 (a number that would make any junior researcher's heart race) that came from a 21-day overlapping regression, and lets you set how many strategies M were tried before this "winner" was found. Predict first, then move the slider.

Commit a prediction before you touch the slider:

Three bars: the Reported naïve t (6.2); after the overlap audit (Newey–West divides it by the SE-inflation factor, here ≈2.7, to ≈2.3 — still "significant"!); and after the search audit (subtract the expected best-of-M null). The dashed line is the naïve 1.96 bar. At M = 1 a real, marginal edge survives; by M = 10 and beyond it is dead. A t of 6 that dies is not a paradox — it is what a heteroskedastic, overlap-inflated, cherry-picked mirage looks like once you audit it.

Sit with the lesson of that widget, because it is the entire quarter in one picture: the same dataset, the same β̂, the same "t = 6.2" — and the honest conclusion swings from "a real edge" to "pure noise" depending only on questions the naïve analyst never asked. The number on the screen was never the evidence. The evidence is the number after you have accounted for how many independent observations you really had and how many things you tried. This is why the mission phrases success as knowing "exactly what you are standing on": the autopsy is how you find the floor.

A worked autopsy (do this in your head before the lab)

The case. A colleague shows you a backtest: signal S predicts the next month's (21-trading-day) return; regressing the overlapping monthly forward return on S, daily, over ~3 years (n ≈ 800) gives β̂ > 0 with a naïve t of 6.2 and they are already sizing the trade. Run the five questions.

Q1 — measurable? 800 rows, yes on paper — but see Q3, the effective n is far smaller. Q4 — heteroskedasticity? Returns cluster in volatility (Lesson 004), so switch to White SE; here it barely moves the t (≈6.2 → ≈6.2), which is itself a clue: the problem is not variance, it is dependence. Q3 — overlap? Consecutive monthly windows share 20 of 21 days, so the errors are massively autocorrelated. Newey–West with L ≈ 21 lags inflates the SE by ≈2.7×, so t → 6.2 / 2.7 ≈ 2.3, and the implied n_eff = n / 2.7² ≈ 110 — you had ~110 independent observations, not 800. Q2 — the search? Ask the colleague: "how many signals did you try before this one?" If the answer is "about 50," the fair bar is E[max of 50] ≈ 2.25, so the surviving margin is 2.3 − 2.25 ≈ 0.05 — gone. Q5 — leakage? Was S standardized using the whole sample's mean and vol? If so, even the 2.3 is optimistic. Verdict: kill it. The β̂ was fine; the t-statistic was a stack of three lies.

That worked case is the exact shape of the lab, and of a real research meeting. Note the discipline: you did not need to be told the flaw. You ran the battery, and the battery found it. That is the difference between a data-miner and a researcher, and it is the difference this checkpoint grades.

Explain it back

Trap — the ways a checkpoint (and a career) is failed. (1) Stopping at the first green light. The naïve t cleared 1.96, so you shipped. The whole point is that 1.96 is the start of the audit, not the end. (2) Fixing the estimate instead of the standard error. Chasing a "better β̂" when the problem was always the SE / the effective sample size. Keep the estimate; fix the inference. (3) Forgetting the trials you did not report. The single most common way real research fools itself: the reported t is the survivor of a silent search, benchmarked against 1.96 as if it were the only test. Always ask "best of how many?" (4) Confusing in-sample fit with edge. A high R² on returns is a red flag, not a trophy — it usually means leakage (Lesson 009). Real edges have tiny R². (5) Regressing levels, not returns. Two unrelated random walks look strongly "related"; the giant t is spurious. Difference your data (this becomes rigorous in Q3, units 021/027). (6) Performing fluency instead of admitting confusion. The meta-trap. If you cannot explain why a t deflated, you have not passed — you have memorized. Say "I don't know yet" and run the test.

The exit criteria (the bar you must clear)

You have passed Unit 010, and may advance to Q2, when you can do all of the following cold — no notes, no re-reading the lessons:

  • Run the lab (labs/0010-checkpoint-spurious-signal.ipynb) end to end: its CHECK cells pass and the EXIT TICKET prints a correct verdict on the planted signal.
  • State the five questions of the audit from memory, and for each, name the failure mode and the test.
  • Explain in one sentence why the point estimate survives but the t-statistic lies under heteroskedasticity, autocorrelation, and selection.
  • Given a claimed t and (a) an overlap horizon and (b) a search count M, produce the honest, deflated t — dividing by the overlap inflation and subtracting the best-of-M bar.
  • Correctly kill or confirm the lab's signal and a second scenario you have not seen (ask your teacher for a fresh one), articulating which of the five questions did the killing.

If any of these is shaky, that is not a failure — it is information. Re-quiz on the specific lesson (say Re-quiz me on Lesson 007) and return. Do not advance on a shaky checkpoint; Q2 (stochastic calculus and pricing) assumes this hygiene is automatic, and it does not get easier to install later.

Check your understanding

Common questions (and honest answers)

“The overlap audit already killed the naïve t. Why run the other four?”
Two reasons. First, in a real notebook you do not know in advance which audit will bite — running only the one you guessed is exactly the un-signposted trap the checkpoint tests. Second, a defensible write-up quantifies all the damage: "the t fell from 6.2 to 2.3 for overlap, and the fair bar given 50 trials is 2.25, so there is no surviving edge" is a far stronger statement than "overlap was a problem." A PM will attack every angle; you want to have closed them all first.
“Isn't subtracting E[max of M] just double-counting if I also Bonferroni-correct?”
They are two encodings of the same idea (control false discoveries under a search), so you use one, not both. Lesson 007's Bonferroni/BH raise the p-value hurdle; the "subtract E[max of M]" haircut lowers the statistic to a surviving margin. The autopsy widget uses the expected-max form because it plots cleanly as a single deflating bar, and because it is the exact spirit of the deflated Sharpe ratio you will meet in Year 2 (unit 055). Pick the tool that fits the write-up; never stack two multiple-testing corrections.
“How would I know M — the number of strategies tried — if I'm auditing someone else's work?”
You ask, and you count generously. The honest M includes every variant silently discarded: parameter grids, alternative signals, universes, look-back windows, and the "I just eyeballed a few charts" trials that never got logged. Researchers systematically under-report M, which is why selection bias is the most common cause of false discoveries. When you cannot pin it down, report the surviving t as a function of M (as the widget does) and let the reader see how fragile the "edge" is.
“White barely changed the t in the worked case. Doesn't that mean robust SEs don't matter?”
No — it means you matched the fix to the violation and learned something. White ≈ classical here says the trouble is not heteroskedasticity; the near-identical result is a diagnostic that points you at autocorrelation instead (where Newey–West then moves the t a lot). On a pure cross-section of noisy small-caps, White would be the one doing the heavy lifting and Newey–West would add little. Run both; which one bites tells you what kind of dependence you are facing.
“I passed the quizzes but the lab feels hard. Am I ready for Q2?”
The lab is the checkpoint; the quizzes are warm-ups. Recognition (multiple choice) is the weakest form of knowing. If you can run the autopsy on the lab's planted signal, explain which question killed it, and reproduce the deflated-t arithmetic on a fresh case from your teacher, you are ready. If not, you have found precisely the lesson to revisit — that is the checkpoint doing its job, not a verdict on you.

Vocabulary you now own

  • Checkpoint — a cumulative, un-signposted exit exam for a quarter; you do not advance until you pass it cold.
  • Statistical hygiene — the standing battery of audits (the five questions) that turn a claimed edge into a defensible one.
  • Spurious-signal autopsy — the procedure: deflate a headline t by overlap (Newey–West), then by the best-of-M search bar, then check heteroskedasticity and leakage.
  • Effective sample size n_eff = n / (SE inflation)² — how many independent observations your overlapping data were really worth.
  • Best-of-M bar / selection haircut — the fair benchmark is E[max of M] under the null, not 1.96, when the t is the winner of a search.

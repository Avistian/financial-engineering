Returns & the Stylized Facts

Lesson 004 · Foundations · ~55 min · has lab

Warm up

Prices are non-stationary and awkward to model directly — their level drifts, their scale changes, and "$5" means something different for a $10 stock than a $1,000 one. Returns are the currency of quant research precisely because they normalize all of that away. But returns are not the tame Gaussian variables intro statistics assumes. Across essentially every liquid asset and era, they obey a set of stubborn empirical regularities called the stylized facts (Cont, 2001).

These facts are not academic trivia. Roughly half of all beginner mistakes — inflated Sharpe ratios, "significant" strategies that die live, risk models that miss crashes — trace directly to pretending returns are IID normal when they are not. Every model you build must respect the stylized facts, and learning to see them in real data is today's skill.

This lesson's single skill: compute log returns and identify the stylized facts in real data — fat tails, absent linear autocorrelation, and volatility clustering.

Simple vs log returns

R_t = (P_t − P_{t−1}) / P_{t−1}   ·   r_t = ln(P_t / P_{t−1}) = ln(1 + R_t)

The simple return R_t is the percentage change. The log return r_t is the natural log of the price ratio. They are nearly identical for small moves (r_t ≈ R_t when R_t is small), but they compose differently, and the difference decides which one a quant reaches for.

Log returns add across time. A k-period log return is simply the sum of the one-period log returns, because logs turn products into sums. Concretely, if a price goes 100 → 110 → 99: r₁ = ln(110/100) = +0.0953, r₂ = ln(99/110) = −0.1054, and their sum −0.0101 = ln(99/100) — exactly the two-period log return. Try that with simple returns and you'd have to multiply (1+R₁)(1+R₂), not add. This additivity is why log returns play nicely with the Brownian-motion and random-walk models of Q2.

Simple returns add across assets. A portfolio's simple return is the weighted sum of its holdings' simple returns — which is why performance and portfolio math often uses simple returns. The rule of thumb: log returns for time-series modeling, simple returns for cross-sectional/portfolio aggregation.

Two subtleties worth banking now. First, the approximation r ≈ R only holds for small moves: a +50% day is R = 0.50 but r = ln(1.5) = 0.405 — noticeably different, so never use the approximation on crash-sized moves. Second, the domains differ: a simple return can't be worse than −100% (a price floors at zero), while a log return is unbounded below. That asymmetry is exactly what makes the lognormal price model of Lesson 005 natural — logs are the symmetric, additive coordinate for a quantity that compounds multiplicatively.

The tension between the "no linear autocorr" and "volatility clustering" rows is the entire game. Direction is nearly unpredictable from past returns alone — the near-zero autocorrelation means yesterday's return tells you almost nothing about the sign of tomorrow's. Yet volatility is quite predictable: big moves cluster, so a turbulent day makes a turbulent tomorrow more likely. Much of quant research is finding the nonlinear or cross-sectional structure that raw autocorrelation misses (that's where signal-based alpha lives), while modeling volatility on a separate track (that's where risk and option pricing live).

Long memory in volatility

Volatility clustering is not a one-day effect. The autocorrelation of |r_t| stays positive for a very long time — hundreds of lags — decaying slowly, like a power law rather than the fast exponential decay a simple model would predict. This "long memory" is why volatility models blend information across many horizons (the HAR-RV model literally sums daily, weekly, and monthly realized volatility). Contrast the sign of returns, whose autocorrelation dies almost immediately: magnitude has a long memory, direction has essentially none. Holding those two facts side by side is the single most useful mental model of return dynamics you can carry into Year 2.

The leverage effect

Volatility responds asymmetrically to the sign of returns: a large drop tends to raise future volatility more than an equally large rise does. The classic (partial) explanation is corporate leverage — as a firm's equity falls, its debt-to-equity ratio rises, making the equity riskier — hence "leverage effect," though behavioral and risk-premium stories also contribute. The practical upshot: symmetric volatility models (plain GARCH) miss this, so Q3 introduces asymmetric variants (GJR-GARCH, EGARCH). It also explains why equity index returns have negative skew: down moves are both larger and beget more turbulence.

Scaling volatility across horizons (and why it's only approximate)

If returns were IID, variance would add across time, so volatility would scale with the square root of the horizon:

σ(T days) ≈ σ(1 day) · √T   ⇒   σ(annual) ≈ σ(daily) · √252

So a stock with 1% daily volatility has roughly 1% × √252 ≈ 16% annualized volatility. This "√t rule" is everywhere — annualizing Sharpe ratios, sizing risk, quoting vol — so it's worth knowing cold. But notice the assumption: it needs returns to be IID with finite, constant variance. Volatility clustering (variance is not constant) and fat tails (higher moments misbehave) mean the rule is an approximation, and worse, using it with Gaussian quantiles to compute Value-at-Risk understates tail risk — the same fat-tail leak, wearing a different hat. Use √t for back-of-envelope scaling; distrust it for the tails.

Aggregational gaussianity — fat tails are a horizon effect

Here's a fact that reconciles "returns are fat-tailed" with "the CLT says sums go normal": as you aggregate returns over longer windows (daily → weekly → monthly), their distribution looks more normal and the excess kurtosis shrinks. Fat tails are largely a high-frequency phenomenon; monthly returns are far closer to Gaussian than one-minute returns. This matters when you choose a model: a daily-bar strategy must take fat tails seriously, while a monthly rebalanced factor portfolio can often lean on near-normal approximations. The stylized facts are not universal constants — they are strongest at short horizons and fade as you zoom out.

How to see them (the lab does this)

import numpy as np
r = np.log(px).diff().dropna()
r.kurtosis()            # >> 0   → fat tails (pandas reports EXCESS kurtosis)
r.autocorr(lag=1)       # ~ 0    → no linear predictability of direction
r.abs().autocorr(lag=1) # > 0    → volatility clustering
r.skew()                # < 0    → negative skew (equity indices)

Four one-liners, four stylized facts. The lab has you run these on a real series and on a simulated Gaussian series of the same mean and variance, so you can see what the normal distribution is missing — its tails are too thin, its |r| autocorrelation is flat, and its skew is zero. It also has you re-aggregate to weekly and monthly and watch the kurtosis fall, confirming aggregational gaussianity with your own eyes.

Trap. Reporting Sharpe ratios and t-statistics as if returns were IID normal. Fat tails and volatility clustering violate both assumptions, inflating apparent significance and making overfit strategies look "statistically real." This is the first leak through which false edges enter — and the reason the deflated Sharpe ratio (Year 2 Q2) exists. Distrust any single-number performance claim that ignores the stylized facts.

Explain it back

Common questions (and honest answers)

“If direction is unpredictable, how does anyone make money?”
"No linear autocorrelation of a single series" is not "no predictability." Edge comes from nonlinear structure, cross-sectional relationships (this stock vs its peers), alternative data, and shorter horizons — plus, separately, from trading volatility itself, which is forecastable. The stylized facts tell you where the easy, naïve edge isn't.
“Aren't fat tails just outliers I should clean out?”
No — the tails are the signal, not noise. Extreme moves are a genuine, repeated property of markets; deleting them is how a risk model comes to fatally understate crash probability. You model the tails (Student-t, EVT), you don't erase them.
“Why not just always use simple returns — they're more intuitive?”
For reporting a single asset's P&L, fine. But for time-series modeling you want additivity across time and compatibility with Brownian models, which log returns give and simple returns don't. Use each where its algebra is clean.
“If returns go Gaussian at long horizons, can't I always just annualize with √t?”
√t scaling is a fine back-of-envelope, but it assumes IID returns. Clustering and fat tails break the assumption, so it misstates risk — especially in the tails, where it understates the chance of a large loss. Use it to get a ballpark, not to set risk limits.

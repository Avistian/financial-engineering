Regression Done Right: OLS, Robust Standard Errors, and When Regression Lies

Lesson 009 · Foundations · ~60 min

Warm up

Regression is the single most-used tool in quantitative research: does this signal predict next period's return? By how much? And — the question that separates a researcher from a data-miner — are we sure the relationship is real, or did we just fit noise? Every factor model, every "signal → return" study, every risk decomposition you will build is a regression underneath. Lesson 008 gave you the geometry of projection; this lesson cashes it in, because ordinary least squares (OLS) regression is projection — the same picture, now doing the work.

This lesson's single skill: run a regression of returns on a signal, read off the slope and its t-statistic, and — crucially — know when that t-statistic is a lie. Because the two things that make markets hard, both of which you already met, are exactly the two things that break the textbook regression formulas: heteroskedasticity (volatility clustering, Lesson 004 — the error variance is not constant) and autocorrelation (overlapping returns and persistent signals — the errors are serially correlated). Under either one, OLS still gives you the right slope, but the naïve standard error is wrong, the t-stat is inflated, and you manufacture a "discovery" that is really a multiple-testing accident waiting to happen (Lesson 007). The fixes — White (heteroskedasticity-robust) and Newey–West (HAC) standard errors — are the payoff, and the lab.

The linear model

Write down what we are assuming. We have n observations. For each one, an outcome yi (say, an asset's return next period) and a row of p predictors xi (signals, factor exposures, a constant). The linear model says the outcome is a weighted sum of the predictors plus an error:

yi = β₀ + β₁ xi1 + … + βp xip + εi  ⇔   y = Xβ + ε

Every symbol, defined:

  • y — the n-vector of outcomes (the thing we want to explain or predict).
  • X — the n × p design matrix: one row per observation, one column per predictor. The first column is usually all 1s, so that β₀ is an intercept (the predicted y when every signal is zero).
  • β — the p-vector of coefficients we want to estimate. βk is the change in y per one-unit change in predictor k, holding the others fixed. In markets, a signal's β is its predictive strength; the intercept on a return regression is the ghost of Lesson 001's alpha.
  • ε — the error (or disturbance): everything about y that the predictors do not capture. This term is where all the trouble — and all the honesty — lives.

We do not know β; we estimate it from data and call the estimate β̂ ("beta hat"). The fitted value is ŷi = xiβ̂ and the residual is ei = yi − ŷi — the model's miss on observation i. Note the distinction we will keep sharp all lesson: ε is the true, unobservable error; e is the observed residual, our estimate of it.

OLS: minimize the squared misses

There are infinitely many lines you could draw through a cloud of points. Ordinary least squares picks the one β̂ that makes the residuals smallest in a specific sense: it minimizes the sum of squared residuals (SSR).

β̂ = argminβ Σi (yi − xiβ)² = argminβ ‖y − Xβ‖²

Why squared, not absolute, misses? Two reasons that matter. First, squaring makes the objective smooth and gives a unique closed-form answer (below). Second — and this is a real modelling choice — squaring punishes large misses disproportionately, so OLS is sensitive to outliers, a fact we return to in the trap. Setting the derivative of the objective to zero gives the normal equations XᵀXβ̂ = Xᵀy, whose solution is the formula you must be able to write from memory:

β̂ = (XᵀX)−1 Xᵀy

For a single predictor plus a constant (the case to internalize), this collapses to something you already know from Lesson 008:

β̂₁ = Cov(x, y) / Var(x) = Σ(xi−x̄)(yi−ȳ) / Σ(xi−x̄)² ,   β̂₀ = ȳ − β̂₁x̄

The slope is the covariance of predictor and outcome, normalized by how much the predictor itself varies. A signal that co-moves strongly with returns but barely moves itself gets a large coefficient; a signal that swings wildly gets its co-movement divided down. And the intercept is pinned so the line passes through the center of mass (x̄, ȳ) — a fact worth remembering.

Worked example (five days, one signal). Signal x = (−2, −1, 0, 1, 2) (say a standardized order-flow imbalance) and next-day returns (in %) y = (−1.5, −0.5, 0.5, 0.5, 2.0). Then x̄ = 0, ȳ = 0.2. Sxy = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) = 3.4 + 0.7 + 0 + 0.3 + 3.6 = 8.0 and Sxx = Σ(x−x̄)² = 4+1+0+1+4 = 10. So β̂₁ = 8.0/10 = 0.80 and β̂₀ = 0.2 − 0.80·0 = 0.20. The fitted line is ŷ = 0.2 + 0.8x. Residuals e = (−0.1, +0.1, +0.3, −0.5, +0.2). Check two things: Σei = 0 and Σxiei = 0 — the residuals are orthogonal to both the constant and the signal. That orthogonality is not a coincidence; it is the whole geometry, next.

OLS is projection (the Lesson 008 payoff)

Here is the picture that makes least squares click. The columns of X span a subspace — the column space, all the vectors you can build as Xβ for some β. The outcome y almost never lies in that subspace (that is what the error is). OLS finds the point in the column space closest to y: it drops a perpendicular from y onto the subspace. The foot of that perpendicular is the fitted vector ŷ, and the perpendicular itself is the residual vector e. In symbols, ŷ = Hy where H = X(XᵀX)−1Xᵀ is the hat matrix (it "puts the hat on" y) — a projection matrix onto the column space. The residual e = (I − H)y is orthogonal to every column of X, which is exactly the Σe = 0 and Σxe = 0 you just verified.

"Closest point in a subspace" is precisely the projection you saw in Lesson 008 when PCA reconstructed a panel from a few components. Least squares is that same operation, and it is why the residual carries no remaining linear information about the predictors: any leftover correlation would mean y was not yet at its closest approach. Drag the slope below and watch the sum of squared residuals bottom out exactly at the projection line:

Teal dots are (signal, return) pairs; the black dot is the center of mass (x̄, ȳ) that every candidate line passes through. Gold is the candidate line; the red stubs are residuals. The readout tracks SSR. It reaches its minimum — and the residuals balance to zero and become orthogonal to x — exactly when the slope equals β̂ = Cov/Var. No other line does better; that is what "projection" means.

How good is the fit? R² and the residual variance

Two summaries of a fit you must read fluently. The residual variance σ̂² = SSR / (n − p) estimates the error variance Var(ε); the divisor n − p (degrees of freedom, p = number of coefficients including the intercept) is the regression cousin of Lesson 006's Bessel correction — we used up p pieces of information fitting the coefficients. The coefficient of determination

R² = 1 − SSR / SST ,   SST = Σ(yi − ȳ)²

is the fraction of the outcome's variance the model explains: R² = 0 means the predictors add nothing beyond the mean, R² = 1 means a perfect fit. In our worked example SST = 6.80 and SSR = 0.40, so R² = 1 − 0.40/6.80 ≈ 0.94 — a suspiciously good fit, because it is a toy. A warning you will need constantly in finance: real return-prediction regressions have tiny R² — an R² of 0.01 (1%) on daily returns can be a genuinely valuable signal. Chasing high R² is a beginner's error; the money is in a small, robust edge, not a high in-sample fit. High R² on returns usually means you have leaked the future into the predictors.

Inference: the coefficient's standard error and t-stat

A slope of 0.80 is worthless without knowing its uncertainty. If we drew a different sample, we would get a different β̂; the spread of β̂ across hypothetical samples is its standard error SE(β̂) — the regression version of Lesson 006's standard error. Under the classical assumptions — errors have mean zero, constant variance σ² (homoskedastic), and are uncorrelated across observations (no autocorrelation) — the sampling variance of the coefficient vector has a clean closed form:

Var(β̂) = σ² (XᵀX)−1  ;   single predictor:   Var(β̂₁) = σ² / Σ(xi−x̄)² = σ² / Sxx

Read the single-predictor formula: your slope is more precise when the error variance σ² is small and when the predictor varies a lot (large Sxx — a signal that spans a wide range pins the slope down better). We do not know σ², so we plug in σ̂² and take the square root to get SE(β̂₁) = √(σ̂²/Sxx). Then, exactly as in Lesson 007, we form the t-statistic

t = β̂₁ / SE(β̂₁)

to test the null "this predictor has no effect" (β₁ = 0). In our worked example σ̂² = SSR/(n−2) = 0.40/3 = 0.133, so SE(β̂₁) = √(0.133/10) = 0.115 and t = 0.80/0.115 ≈ 6.9 — comfortably past the ±1.96 bar. This is the number a PM will grill you on, and it is exactly the number the next two sections show you how to compute wrong without noticing.

The theorem underneath all this is Gauss–Markov: under those classical assumptions, OLS is BLUE — the Best (minimum-variance) Linear Unbiased Estimator. The word doing the heavy lifting is "under those assumptions." Markets violate two of them routinely, and when they do, the β̂ stays unbiased but the SE formula above quietly breaks.

Commit a prediction before the reveal:

When regression lies I: heteroskedasticity

Heteroskedasticity (Greek: "different scatter") means the error variance is not constant — Var(εi) = σi² changes across observations. This is the default in finance, not the exception: volatility clusters (Lesson 004), so calm days have small errors and turbulent days have large ones; and cross-sectionally, small-cap or high-beta names are simply noisier than blue chips. The classical formula Var(β̂₁) = σ²/Sxx assumed one σ² for everyone. When that is false, two things happen — and only one of them is obvious:

  • The coefficient β̂ is still unbiased and consistent. OLS does not need homoskedasticity to find the right slope on average. Do not throw out your point estimate.
  • The classical standard error is wrong — often badly, and usually too small when the loudest errors sit on the highest-leverage (most extreme-x) points. A too-small SE means a too-big t-stat: you declare significance that is not there.

The fix is the White (heteroskedasticity-robust, "HC") standard error (White, 1980). Instead of assuming one σ², it estimates each observation's error variance by its own squared residual ei² and plugs that into a "sandwich" formula. For a single predictor:

VarWhite(β̂₁) = Σ(xi−x̄)² ei² / (Sxx)²

Compare it to the classical σ̂²/Sxx = [Σei²/(n−2)]/Sxx: the classical version pulls the residuals out as one average variance, while White keeps each ei² attached to its own leverage weight (xi−x̄)². If big residuals coincide with extreme x, White is larger — and correct. In practice you almost never hand-derive this; you pass one argument (cov_type='HC1' in statsmodels, vcovHC in R). But you must know why and when. Slide the heteroskedasticity up and watch the honest SE pull away from the naïve one:

The gold band is ±2σi around the fitted line — it fans out with |x| as you raise k. The slope β̂ stays unbiased (it just gets noisier). But the classical SE lags the truth while the White SE tracks it: at high k the robust SE is meaningfully larger, so the naïve t-stat is overstated. The naïve regression would call this signal "significant" more often than it deserves.

When regression lies II: autocorrelation & Newey–West

The second violation is subtler and, in finance, more dangerous: autocorrelated errors — εt is correlated with εt−1. The classic culprit is overlapping returns. Suppose you regress the next 21-day (monthly) return on a signal, but you do it every day. Consecutive observations share 20 of their 21 days, so consecutive errors are mechanically almost identical — hugely positively autocorrelated. Persistent regressors (a slow-moving valuation signal) do the same thing. You think you have thousands of independent data points; you effectively have a handful.

The consequence mirrors heteroskedasticity but is usually worse: β̂ stays unbiased, but positive autocorrelation makes the classical SE far too small, so t-stats can be inflated by multiples. A "t = 4" monthly-overlap result can be a genuine "t = 1.5" once you account for the overlap. The general fix is the Newey–West standard error (Newey & West, 1987), a HAC estimator — Heteroskedasticity- And- Autocorrelation- Consistent. It generalizes White by adding in the autocovariances of the residuals out to L lags, down-weighted by the Bartlett kernel wl = 1 − l/(L+1) (the taper is what guarantees the estimated variance stays non-negative):

LRV = γ̂₀ + 2 Σl=1L (1 − l/(L+1)) γ̂l

where γ̂l is the lag-l autocovariance of the (leverage-weighted) residuals and LRV is the long-run variance that replaces the naïve one. For the simplest case — the standard error of a mean when the series is AR(1) with autocorrelation φ — there is a clean closed form for how badly the naïve SE understates: the true variance is inflated by the factor (1+φ)/(1−φ), so the SE (and the honest t-stat correction) scales by its square root. At φ = 0.5 that is √3 ≈ 1.7×; at φ = 0.8 it is √9 = 3×. Move φ and watch the "sticky" error path and the SE gap:

The teal line is the error series over time; as φ rises it becomes visibly persistent (long excursions away from zero — the signature of overlap). The naïve SE barely moves because it only sees the one-period variance; the Newey–West SE climbs to track the true (inflated) SE. When Newey–West falls a little short of "true" at very high φ, that is the finite-lag truncation being honest — you need enough lags L to span the overlap.

Practical rule of thumb: with h-period overlapping returns, use at least L ≈ h lags (many use the automatic L ≈ 4(n/100)2/9 rule). The one-line takeaway you must carry into every backtest: if your observations overlap or your errors persist, a naïve t-stat is not evidence — recompute it Newey–West before you believe any number.

Explain it back

Trap — the ways a regression lies to you. (1) Heteroskedasticity. Non-constant error variance (vol clustering, noisy small-caps) leaves β̂ unbiased but breaks the classical SE — usually making the t-stat too big. Fix: White/HC robust SEs, always, on financial data. (2) Autocorrelation / overlapping returns. Serially correlated errors (overlapping horizons, persistent signals) shrink your effective sample size and inflate t-stats by multiples. Fix: Newey–West (HAC) with enough lags; better yet, avoid overlap by sampling non-overlapping periods. (3) Spurious regression. Regressing one non-stationary (trending, unit-root) series on another manufactures a huge R² and a giant t-stat between things that have nothing to do with each other — the reason we regress returns, not prices. This is previewed here and made rigorous in Q3 (stationarity & cointegration, units 021, 027). (4) Omitted-variable bias. Leave out a predictor that belongs in the model and is correlated with the ones you kept, and the coefficients you kept absorb its effect — now β̂ is biased, not just imprecise. "Controlling for the market" exists because of this. (5) Multicollinearity. Two nearly-redundant predictors (correlated signals) make XᵀX nearly singular; individual coefficients get huge, unstable, and sign-flipping SEs even though the joint fit is fine. Echo of Lesson 008's noise-eigenvalue problem. (6) Influential outliers. Because OLS squares residuals, one bad tick or a crash day can swing the whole line. Always look at the scatter, not just the coefficients. (7) Look-ahead / leakage. Standardizing, winsorizing, or picking the signal using the full sample bleeds the future into X. Same discipline as Lessons 007–008, wearing a regression hat.

Common questions (and honest answers)

“If robust standard errors are almost always right, why not just use them everywhere and forget the classical ones?”
Largely, do. On real financial data you should default to White (for cross-sections) or Newey–West (for time series / overlap). The one caveat: robust SEs are large-sample justified, so in very small samples they can be noisy or even slightly anti-conservative (HC1/HC3 corrections help). They also cannot rescue a biased β̂ — if you have omitted-variable bias or leakage, no SE fixes that. Robust SEs cure the precision lie, not the estimate lie.
“Heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation both leave β̂ unbiased. So why do I care — I just want the coefficient.”
Because a coefficient without a trustworthy standard error is not a research result; it is a number. Your whole job (the mission) is to say "this edge is real" and survive a skeptical audit. The t-stat is the claim; if it is inflated 3× by overlap you will confidently ship noise, and it will die out of sample. The point estimate tells you the size of the effect; the honest SE tells you whether you are allowed to believe it exists.
“Is OLS the same as the correlation or the beta I keep hearing about?”
They are cousins. A single-predictor slope is β̂ = Cov(x,y)/Var(x); the correlation is ρ = Cov(x,y)/(σxσy) — same numerator, different normalizer, so β̂ = ρ · σy/σx. "Market beta" is literally the slope of a stock's returns regressed on the market's returns. And R² in a single regression is exactly ρ². Regression, correlation, and beta are three views of the same covariance.
“Why regress returns on the signal and not prices on prices?”
Because prices are non-stationary (they wander), and regressing one wandering series on another produces a spurious relationship: high R², high t-stat, zero meaning (trap 3). Two unrelated random walks will look strongly "related" in levels. Returns are (nearly) stationary, so their regression is interpretable. The formal machinery — unit roots, cointegration — is Q3; for now, the rule is "regress returns, difference your data, be suspicious of any regression on raw price levels."
“How many lags should Newey–West use?”
Enough to span the dependence. With h-period overlapping returns, the errors have an MA(h−1) structure, so use at least L = h − 1 lags; a common automatic choice is L ≈ 4(n/100)2/9. Too few lags and you under-correct (t-stat still inflated); far too many and the estimate gets noisy. When in doubt, check that your conclusion is stable as you vary L — if "significance" evaporates at a reasonable lag count, it was never there.

Vocabulary you now own

  • Linear model y = Xβ + ε; design matrix X, coefficients β, error ε vs. observed residual e.
  • OLS: β̂ = (XᵀX)−1Xᵀy minimizes SSR; one predictor β̂₁ = Cov(x,y)/Var(x).
  • OLS as projection: ŷ = Hy, hat matrix H = X(XᵀX)−1Xᵀ; residuals orthogonal to the column space.
  • R² = 1 − SSR/SST; residual variance σ̂² = SSR/(n−p); Gauss–Markov / BLUE.
  • Standard error and t-stat of a coefficient; classical Var(β̂) = σ²(XᵀX)−1.
  • Heteroskedasticity → White / HC robust SE; autocorrelation → Newey–West (HAC) SE, Bartlett kernel, long-run variance.
  • Traps: spurious regression, omitted-variable bias, multicollinearity, outliers/leverage, leakage.

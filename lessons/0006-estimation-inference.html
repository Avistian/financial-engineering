Estimation & Inference: From Data to Honest Uncertainty

Lesson 006 · Foundations · ~60 min

Warm up

Lesson 005 gave you the objects — moments, distributions, covariance, conditional expectation. But in real work you never know those quantities; you have a finite, noisy sample and must estimate them. This lesson is about the single discipline that separates a quant researcher from someone who just fits models: every number you report from data is itself a random quantity, so it must come with an honest error bar. A mean return, a volatility, a correlation, a Sharpe ratio — each is a guess, and the guess has uncertainty you are obligated to quantify before you stake capital or your reputation on it.

This lesson's single skill: turn a finite sample into (1) a point estimate and (2) a defensible statement of how uncertain that estimate is — using the bias/variance vocabulary, maximum likelihood, confidence intervals, and the bootstrap. The mission-critical payoff is the Sharpe-ratio confidence interval in this unit's lab: the difference between "my strategy has a Sharpe of 1.3" and "my strategy has a Sharpe of 1.3, 95% CI [0.2, 2.4]" is the difference between a data-mined mirage and a claim you can defend.

The setup: estimand, estimator, estimate

Three words that are easy to blur and worth keeping crisp:

  • The estimand (or parameter) is the unknown, fixed truth you care about — the population mean return μ, the true volatility σ, the strategy's true Sharpe. Call it θ (theta) in general. It is a fixed number, not random; you just don't know it.
  • An estimator θ̂ ("theta-hat") is a rule — a function of the data — for guessing θ. "Take the sample average" is an estimator. Because it is computed from a random sample, the estimator is itself a random variable: draw a different sample and you get a different value.
  • An estimate is the single number the estimator spits out on the particular data you happen to have. "0.42" is an estimate.

The whole subject lives in one realization: since θ̂ is random, it has a distribution — the sampling distribution — and everything below (bias, variance, standard error, confidence intervals) is just a way of describing that distribution. The hat notation (θ̂, μ̂, σ̂) is universal shorthand for "an estimate of the thing under the hat"; we use it throughout.

Bias, variance, and the standard error

Two properties summarize how good an estimator is. Bias is how far off it is on average: Bias(θ̂) = E[θ̂] − θ. An estimator is unbiased if its average over many samples lands exactly on the truth (bias 0). Variance is how much the estimate jumps around from sample to sample: Var(θ̂) = E[(θ̂ − E[θ̂])²]. Low bias means "aimed at the target"; low variance means "tight grouping."

The standard error (SE) is simply the standard deviation of the estimator's sampling distribution — the square root of its variance. It is the number you put after a "±". The canonical case is the sample mean of n independent observations, each with standard deviation σ:

θ̂ = x̄ = (x₁ + … + xₙ) / n   ⟹   SE(x̄) = σ / √n

Worked example. Daily returns with a true volatility of σ = 1%, one year of data (n = 252). The standard error of the estimated mean daily return is 0.01 / √252 = 0.01 / 15.87 ≈ 0.00063, i.e. about 0.063% per day. Now notice the trap that is coming: a typical true mean daily return might be a few basis points — smaller than its own error bar. That is why estimating the drift (mean return) of an asset is notoriously hard, while estimating its volatility is comparatively easy. It is a fact about standard errors, not about your cleverness.

Why sample variance divides by n − 1. When you estimate the variance with σ̂² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n − 1), the n − 1 (not n) is Bessel's correction. Dividing by n is biased downward because you measured deviations from the sample mean x̄ — which is itself fitted to the data and so sits closer to the points than the true mean does. You "used up" one degree of freedom estimating x̄, so only n − 1 independent pieces of spread information remain. Dividing by n − 1 corrects the bias exactly.

The bias–variance decomposition of error

Bias and variance are not competing virtues you pick between arbitrarily — they add up, in a precise way, to total expected error. The standard scorecard is mean squared error (MSE), the average squared distance between the estimate and the truth, and it splits cleanly:

MSE(θ̂) = E[(θ̂ − θ)²] = Bias(θ̂)² + Var(θ̂)

Read it as a target-shooting picture. The bullseye is the true θ. Each shot is one estimate from one sample. Bias is how far the center of your shot cluster sits from the bullseye; variance is how spread out the cluster is. Total error blends both.

Four estimators on the same target. The bullseye is the true value; gold dots are estimates (one per sample); the red ring is their average (its gap from the bullseye is bias). Re-sample to see that the character of each panel is stable even as individual shots move. The bottom-left panel — precise but systematically off — is the dangerous one: it looks reliable and is confidently wrong.

Worked example (why a biased estimator can win). Suppose estimator A is unbiased but noisy: bias 0, variance 0.25, so MSE = 0 + 0.25 = 0.25. Estimator B is slightly biased but much steadier: bias 0.2 (so bias² = 0.04), variance 0.09, so MSE = 0.04 + 0.09 = 0.13. B is better despite being biased — its MSE is roughly half. This is not a curiosity; it is the entire logic behind shrinkage estimators (Ledoit–Wolf covariance shrinkage, ridge regression, hierarchical risk parity) that you will meet in Year 2–3. On noisy financial data, deliberately accepting a little bias to kill a lot of variance is often the winning trade. "Unbiased" is not a synonym for "best."

Consistency and the √n rate

An estimator is consistent if it converges to the truth as the sample grows — pour in more data and θ̂ → θ. This is the estimator-level version of the Law of Large Numbers from Lesson 005. Consistency is a low bar (it only says "eventually right with infinite data"), but it is the minimum you should demand: an inconsistent estimator can be biased no matter how much data you feed it.

The more useful question is the rate: how fast does the error shrink? From SE = σ/√n the answer is the √n rate, and it is humbling. To halve your error bar you need four times the data; to shrink it by 10× you need 100× the data. This is exactly the Central Limit Theorem's σ/√n from Lesson 005, now read as a budget: edges in markets are small, and the √n wall is the reason proving a small edge is real demands so much history — often more history than any single regime actually gives you.

Effective sample size < row count. The √n formula assumes independent observations. Financial data is autocorrelated (volatility clusters; overlapping windows share data), so your effective number of independent observations is smaller than your row count — sometimes far smaller. If returns have positive autocorrelation ρ, the variance of the mean inflates roughly by a factor of (1 + ρ)/(1 − ρ). Naïvely using σ/√n with the raw row count therefore reports an error bar that is too small — the single most common way quants fool themselves about significance. Hold that thought; it is this lesson's trap.

Maximum likelihood: the workhorse estimator

So far "take the average" appeared by intuition. Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) is the general recipe that produces good estimators for almost any parametric model. The idea is a flip of perspective. The likelihood is the probability of the data you actually observed, viewed as a function of the unknown parameter: L(θ) = P(data | θ). The MLE is the parameter value that makes your observed data most probable — the peak of that function:

θ̂MLE = argmaxθ L(θ) = argmaxθ log L(θ)

We almost always maximize the log-likelihood ℓ(θ) = log L(θ) instead of L itself, for two reasons: probabilities of many independent observations multiply, and logs turn products into sums (easier to differentiate and numerically stabler — multiplying thousands of small probabilities underflows to zero). Because log is increasing, the location of the peak is unchanged.

Worked example (the up-day probability). Model each day as up or down, up with unknown probability p (a Bernoulli trial). You observe n days, k of them up. The probability of that exact data is L(p) = pk(1 − p)n−k, so the log-likelihood is ℓ(p) = k·ln p + (n − k)·ln(1 − p). Set the derivative to zero: ℓ′(p) = k/p − (n − k)/(1 − p) = 0, which solves to

p̂MLE = k / n   (the sample fraction of up-days)

MLE recovers the obvious estimator here — the point is that the same machinery derives estimators when the answer is not obvious (GARCH volatility parameters, a Student-t's degrees of freedom, a logistic regression's coefficients). MLE comes with a package of large-sample guarantees that make it the default:

  • Consistency — as n → ∞, θ̂MLE → θ.
  • Asymptotic normality — for large n the estimator is approximately normal around the truth, which is what lets us write a "± z·SE" interval at all.
  • Equivariance (invariance) — the MLE of a function of θ is that function of the MLE: if p̂ is the MLE of p, then g(p̂) is the MLE of g(p). Handy when the thing you report (say, annualized Sharpe) is a transform of the parameters you fit.

Where does the standard error of an MLE come from? From the curvature of the log-likelihood at its peak, formalized as the Fisher information. A sharply peaked likelihood means the data strongly rule out nearby parameter values — lots of information, small standard error. A flat likelihood means many values explain the data almost equally well — little information, large standard error. Concretely, the SE is roughly 1/√(Fisher information). For the Bernoulli case the Fisher information is n / (p(1 − p)), giving SE(p̂) ≈ √(p̂(1 − p̂)/n) — the familiar poll margin-of-error, and it shrinks at the √n rate we just met. Slide n below and watch the peak sharpen (information grows), the peak settle onto the truth (consistency), and the interval narrow (asymptotic normality):

Likelihood of the up-day probability p from n Bernoulli days (true p = 0.55, dashed gold; MLE p̂ = k/n, teal; shaded 95% interval). Small n: a broad hill and a wide interval — the data barely pin down p. Large n: a sharp spike and a tight interval. Curvature at the peak is the Fisher information.

Confidence intervals: what "95%" actually means

A point estimate without an interval is half an answer. A confidence interval (CI) is a range, computed from the data, designed to contain the true parameter with a stated frequency. The textbook construction, when the estimator is approximately normal, is:

θ̂ ± z · SE   (z = 1.96 for 95%, z = 2.58 for 99%)

Here z = 1.96 is the value that cuts off the central 95% of a standard normal distribution. So a 95% CI for a mean is x̄ ± 1.96·σ/√n. Simple — but the interpretation is the part almost everyone gets wrong, and getting it right is a load-bearing idea for the rest of the mission.

What 95% confidence means: if you repeated the whole experiment many times, drawing a fresh sample and building a fresh interval each time, about 95% of those intervals would contain the true parameter. The randomness is in the interval, not in the parameter. The parameter is a fixed (unknown) constant; it does not have a 95% "chance" of being anywhere.

Twenty 95% intervals, each from a fresh sample; the true value is the dashed vertical line. About 19 of 20 cross it (teal); roughly one misses (red) — not because the truth moved, but by unlucky draw. Draw new batches and the long-run coverage hovers near 95%. That long-run coverage is the definition.

The seductive misreading is "there's a 95% probability the true Sharpe is in [0.2, 2.4]." Once you have this particular interval, the true Sharpe is either in it or not — there is no probability left; the 95% describes the procedure's long-run hit rate, not this one interval. (The statement you want to make — a probability about the parameter itself — is a Bayesian credible interval, which requires a prior; we meet Bayesian machinery later. Keep the two straight.)

The bootstrap: an error bar without a formula

The ±z·SE recipe needs a formula for the standard error and an approximate-normal sampling distribution. For a mean, fine. For a Sharpe ratio — a ratio of two estimated, correlated quantities (mean over standard deviation) — the closed-form SE is messy and leans on assumptions (i.i.d., often normal returns) that markets violate. The bootstrap sidesteps the algebra entirely, and it is one of the most important practical tools in this whole track.

The engine is the plug-in principle. The reason we cannot compute the true sampling distribution is that we do not have the true population to draw fresh samples from. The bootstrap's move: use the sample itself as a stand-in for the population. The observed data, weighted equally, is the empirical distribution — the best nonparametric guess of the population you have. So to imitate "draw a fresh sample of size n," you resample with replacement from your data: draw n observations, allowing repeats, so each bootstrap sample reshuffles which points appear and how often. The recipe:

  1. From your n observations, draw n with replacement — one bootstrap sample.
  2. Compute your statistic (here, the Sharpe ratio) on that bootstrap sample.
  3. Repeat B times (say B = 1000–10000), collecting B values.
  4. The spread of those values estimates the statistic's sampling distribution. Its standard deviation is the bootstrap standard error; its 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles are a 95% percentile confidence interval.

One fixed sample of 30 returns (top). "Resample once" draws 30 with replacement — repeats stack taller, some points are skipped — and drops that resample's Sharpe onto the histogram. "Add 1000 resamples" fills the bootstrap distribution and marks the 2.5–97.5 percentile CI (dashed red) around the point estimate (teal). No normality assumed anywhere.

Worked example (the Sharpe CI you will build in the lab). Say 36 monthly returns give a sample Sharpe of 0.25 per month, ≈ 0.87 annualized. Is that "good"? Without an interval you cannot say. Bootstrap 10000 resamples, annualize each, and you might find a 95% CI of roughly [0.1, 1.6] — a range so wide it includes values near zero. The honest conclusion is not "Sharpe 0.87" but "I cannot rule out that this strategy has essentially no edge, given only three years of data." That is precisely the kind of self-skepticism the mission is built to instill, and the √n wall is why: three years is simply not many independent monthly observations.

Explain it back

Trap — the error bar that lies (and the bootstrap that lies with it). Both the classical σ/√n interval and the plain bootstrap above assume observations are independent. Financial data breaks that: returns cluster in volatility, overlapping labels share information, and strategy P&L is autocorrelated. When observations are positively dependent, your effective sample size is smaller than the row count, so every i.i.d. error bar — formula or bootstrap — is too narrow, and t-stats and Sharpe "significance" are overstated. Two fixes you will meet: the block bootstrap (resample contiguous blocks of time, not individual points, to preserve dependence) and, for the Sharpe specifically, Lo's autocorrelation-adjusted standard error. And even a perfect single-strategy interval is not enough: if you tried 100 strategies and reported the best, its CI must be corrected for multiple testing — the subject of Lesson 007. A confidence interval is only honest if the assumptions behind it hold; naming and checking those assumptions is the job.

Common questions (and honest answers)

“Isn't an unbiased estimator always the one to use?”
No. What you usually care about is total error (MSE = bias² + variance), and a slightly biased estimator with much lower variance can have smaller MSE — the whole rationale for shrinkage (Ledoit–Wolf, ridge). On noisy financial data, trading a little bias for a lot of variance reduction is frequently the right call. "Unbiased" is a property, not a goal.
“Does a 95% CI mean the true value has a 95% chance of being inside it?”
Not in the frequentist sense used here. The parameter is fixed; the interval is random. "95%" is the long-run fraction of such intervals that would cover the truth over many repetitions. The probability-about-the-parameter statement you may want is a Bayesian credible interval, which needs a prior and is a different object.
“If the bootstrap makes no assumptions, why not use it for everything?”
It makes fewer distributional assumptions, but it still assumes your resampling scheme matches how the data were generated. The plain (i.i.d.) bootstrap assumes independence, which financial time series violate — so it understates uncertainty just like the formula does. Use a block bootstrap (or model-based resampling) to respect dependence, and remember the bootstrap can be poor for extreme quantiles and very small samples.
“Why is estimating the mean return so much harder than the volatility?”
Because the mean's signal is tiny relative to its noise. A daily drift of a few basis points sits under a standard error of order σ/√n that is often larger than the drift itself, while volatility is estimated from squared moves that accumulate information much faster. This asymmetry — direction hard, magnitude easier — echoes the stylized facts of Lesson 004 and shapes what strategies are even estimable.
“Where does maximum likelihood get its standard error from?”
From the curvature of the log-likelihood at its peak — the Fisher information. Sharp peak ⇒ lots of information ⇒ small SE; flat peak ⇒ little information ⇒ large SE. Formally SE ≈ 1/√(Fisher information), and it shrinks at the √n rate.

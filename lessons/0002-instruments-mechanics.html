Instruments & Market Mechanics

Lesson 002 · Foundations · ~55 min

Warm up

You start from near-zero finance, so this lesson buys you the vocabulary of what actually trades and how an intention becomes a filled position. Every model you build later prices, forecasts, or trades one of these instruments; if the instrument is fuzzy in your head, the model built on it will be fuzzy too. This is deliberately concrete — the abstractions (Itô, Black-Scholes) only earn their keep once you can picture the thing being priced.

This lesson's single skill: describe the four core instrument classes and the anatomy of a trade — long vs short, bid vs ask, and how an order turns into a fill and a P&L.

The four core instruments

Equity (stock)An ownership share of a company (a claim on its assets and future cash flows)Linear: you gain/lose ~1:1 with the price; may pay dividends
FuturesA standardized, exchange-traded contract to buy/sell an asset at a future date and agreed priceLinear, leveraged; marked-to-market daily; used for hedging & directional bets

ETFA fund holding a basket of assets that itself trades like a single stockCheap, liquid exposure to an index/sector via create/redeem arbitrage

That table is the map; the next four subsections are the territory. Each instrument has a mechanical detail that trips up newcomers and shows up later in pricing or backtests, so we take them one at a time.

Equity — and the details that break naïve backtests

A share is a claim on a company. Two mechanical facts matter for a QR. First, dividends: when a company pays a dividend, the share price drops by roughly the dividend on the ex-dividend date — not because anything went wrong, but because that cash left the firm. A backtest on raw prices will read that drop as a loss unless you use total-return (dividend-adjusted) prices. Second, corporate actions like stock splits: a 2-for-1 split halves the price and doubles the share count with no economic change. Unadjusted data shows a spurious 50% "crash." Professional data is adjusted for both; knowing they exist is how you catch a data bug before it becomes a fake signal.

Futures — leverage, mark-to-market, and the roll

A future is a standardized promise to exchange an asset at a set price on a set date. Three properties define it. It is leveraged: you post a small margin (say 5–10% of notional) rather than the full value, so a 1% move in the underlying is a large percentage move on your posted cash. It is marked-to-market daily: gains and losses are settled into your account every day, so you can't quietly accumulate a huge unrealized loss — a margin call forces you to top up or close. And it expires: to keep a position beyond expiry you must roll — close the expiring contract and open the next month's. If further-dated contracts are more expensive (contango), rolling steadily costs you; if cheaper (backwardation), rolling earns. This roll yield is a real, sometimes dominant, component of returns for commodity and volatility products — and a classic hidden cost in a backtest that pretends a single "futures price" persists forever.

Options — moneyness, intrinsic value, and time value

An option is the pivot of the course. The buyer pays a premium up front for the right (not obligation) to transact at the strike K by expiry T. Its price decomposes into two pieces:

premium = intrinsic value + time value

Intrinsic value is what you'd get exercising now: max(S−K,0) for a call. Time value is everything else — the extra you'll pay because the price might move favorably before expiry. Time value is largest when the option is near the money and shrinks to zero at expiry (the option "decays"). The jargon for where the price sits relative to the strike is moneyness: a call is in-the-money (ITM) if S > K, at-the-money (ATM) if S ≈ K, and out-of-the-money (OTM) if S < K (reversed for puts). Because the payoff depends on how much the price might move, an option's value depends on volatility — the single deepest reason options need the stochastic-calculus machinery of Q2, where the Black-Scholes model makes "time value" precise.

ETF — a basket that trades like a stock

An ETF holds a basket (say, the 500 stocks of an index) but trades as one ticker. Why does its price track the value of its holdings (its NAV) so closely? Because of an arbitrage mechanism: authorized participants can create new ETF shares by delivering the basket, or redeem shares for the basket. If the ETF trades above NAV, they create and sell (pushing price down); if below, they buy and redeem (pushing price up). That create/redeem loop keeps price ≈ NAV and is a clean, real-world example of arbitrage enforcing a price relationship — a pattern you'll see again in put-call parity and no-arbitrage pricing.

Equities and futures have linear payoffs — a $1 move in the underlying is a $1 move in your position (times contract size). Options are non-linear, and that single distinction reorganizes how you must think about risk.

Worked payoffs: a call and a put

Take a call with strike K = 100, bought for a premium of 3. At expiry the intrinsic value is max(S_T − K, 0), and your profit is that minus the premium:

  • If S_T = 110: intrinsic = max(10, 0) = 10, so profit = 10 − 3 = +7.
  • If S_T = 95: intrinsic = max(−5, 0) = 0, so you lose only the premium: −3.

Now a put with strike K = 100, premium 4, payoff max(K − S_T, 0) — it profits when the price falls:

  • If S_T = 90: intrinsic = max(10, 0) = 10, so profit = 10 − 4 = +6.
  • If S_T = 105: intrinsic = max(−5, 0) = 0, so you lose the premium: −4.

Notice the asymmetry in both: your loss is capped at the premium, your upside is open (or, for the put, large). If you sketch profit against S_T, each is a flat line at −premium that kinks upward at the strike — the "hockey stick." That kink is non-linearity, and it's why you can't price an option by just guessing the average future price: two futures with the same mean but different volatility give the same expected stock payoff but very different option payoffs.

Long, short, and the two prices

To go long is to buy, profiting if the price rises. To go short is to sell something you have borrowed, profiting if it falls — you sell high now and buy it back cheaper later to return it (paying a borrow fee in between). Shorting is how quants express negative views and build market-neutral books, but it carries a nasty asymmetry we'll flag below.

At any instant the market shows not one price but two:

bid = highest price a buyer will pay   |   ask (offer) = lowest price a seller will accept

The gap between them is the bid-ask spread; the midpoint is the mid price. If you want to trade immediately, you cross the spread — buying at the ask or selling at the bid — and you pay roughly half the spread relative to the mid, every single time. This is a real, recurring cost, not an accounting footnote, and Lesson 003 plus the whole microstructure quarter dissect it.

Before reading on, commit a prediction:

The answer matters for risk: a long position can lose at most what you paid (the price can't go below zero), but a short position's loss is unbounded — the price you must buy back at can rise without limit. Borrow 100 shares and sell at $50 (proceeds $5,000); if the stock falls to $40 you buy back for $4,000 and keep ~$1,000 (less borrow cost). But if it rallies to $80, you must buy back for $8,000 — a $3,000 loss, and rising. There's a second hazard: to short you must first borrow the shares (a "locate"), and the lender can recall them or the borrow fee can spike. A short squeeze — a rising price forcing shorts to buy back, pushing the price higher still — is the extreme case. Short risk is asymmetric and operationally fiddly, and forgetting that has ended careers.

Leverage and margin

Leverage is controlling more notional than the cash you post. Futures embed it (small margin, large contract), and you can also borrow to buy equities (buying "on margin"). Leverage scales both gains and losses: at 5× leverage, a 2% adverse move wipes 10% of your capital. Because futures mark to market daily, a run of losses triggers a margin call — post more cash or be liquidated, often at the worst possible moment. This is why a QR never reports raw return without reporting the risk (volatility, leverage) taken to get it: doubling leverage doubles return and doubles risk, so it is not skill. The Sharpe ratio exists precisely to make leverage-neutral comparisons.

Anatomy of a trade

intention  →  order  →  exchange matching engine  →  fill(s)  →  position  →  P&L
                 │
          market order  (take liquidity now, pay the spread)
          limit order   (post a price, wait, maybe get filled)

A market order demands immediacy: it crosses the spread and removes resting liquidity from the book — you are a taker. A limit order posts a price and waits, offering liquidity to others — you are a maker, and you may never get filled. This taking-vs-making distinction drives execution cost, underpins market-making as a business, and returns in force in Year 3 Q2. Many venues even price it directly: takers pay a fee, makers get a rebate.

Beyond these two, a few order types recur and are worth recognizing now:

  • Stop order — becomes a market order once a trigger price is hit (used for stop-losses); note it does not guarantee your trigger price, since it then walks the book.
  • Marketable limit — a limit priced aggressively enough to fill immediately, capping how far you'll cross.
  • IOC (immediate-or-cancel) — fill what you can right now, cancel the rest.
  • FOK (fill-or-kill) — fill the whole order at once or cancel it entirely.

You don't need to memorize the full zoo, but internalize the axis they all vary along: how much immediacy do I demand, and how much price am I willing to give up for it? That trade-off is the seed of optimal execution.

Notation you'll reuse

  • S_t — price of the underlying asset at time t
  • K — strike price of an option; T — expiry
  • Call payoff at expiry: max(S_T − K, 0); put: max(K − S_T, 0)
  • Notional — the total face value of a position (not the cash you put up)
  • Moneyness — ITM / ATM / OTM, the price's position relative to the strike
Trap. Treating "the price" as a single number. There is no single price — there is a bid, an ask, and a stream of trades. Backtests that assume you buy and sell at the mid (or worse, the daily close) with no spread or impact are the most common way beginners overstate returns. A close second: using unadjusted price data, so dividends and splits masquerade as returns. We fix costs properly in the execution quarter, but start distrusting "the price" and "the raw price series" now.

Explain it back

Common questions (and honest answers)

“Aren't options just gambling?”
Used blindly, sure. But an option is a precise bet on volatility and time, and its non-linear payoff lets you shape risk in ways stock alone cannot (capping downside, hedging a book). Pricing them correctly is a rigorous science — the core of Q2 — not a coin flip.
“Why would a quant ever short — isn't it needlessly risky?”
Shorting is how you express a negative view and, more importantly, how you build a market-neutral book (long the cheap, short the expensive) that profits from relative value while hedging out the market's overall move. The unbounded-loss asymmetry is real and must be risk-managed, not avoided outright.
“Can't I just backtest on daily closing prices?”
You can, and it's a common first step — but the close is one number that ignores the spread and market impact, so any strategy that trades often will look far better on paper than in reality. Treat close-only backtests as an upper bound to be discounted, never as the truth.
“If futures track the underlying, why does the roll matter?”
Because you never hold "the future" forever — contracts expire, and you roll to the next one at whatever price it trades. In contango you sell low (expiring) and buy high (next month) repeatedly, bleeding roll yield. For products like volatility ETFs, that bleed can dominate the headline exposure entirely.

Being able to draw those two kinked lines and label moneyness from memory is worth more than re-reading the chapter.

Vocabulary you now own

  • Long / short — profit if price rises / falls (short = sell borrowed, unbounded loss).
  • Bid / ask / spread / mid — the two live prices, their gap, and its midpoint.
  • Maker / taker — provide liquidity (limit) vs demand it (market).
  • Premium = intrinsic + time value; moneyness (ITM/ATM/OTM).
  • Margin / leverage / margin call; roll, contango, backwardation.
  • Notional — face value of a position, distinct from cash posted.

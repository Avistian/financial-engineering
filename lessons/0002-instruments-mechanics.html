Instruments & Market Mechanics

Lesson 002 · Foundations · ~40 min

You start with near-zero finance, so this lesson buys you the vocabulary of what actually trades. Every model later prices, forecasts, or trades one of these instruments; if the instrument is fuzzy, the model is fuzzy.

This lesson's single skill: describe the four core instrument classes and the anatomy of a trade — long/short, bid/ask, and how an intention becomes a fill.

The four core instruments

InstrumentWhat it isPayoff / key idea
Equity (stock)Ownership share of a companyLinear: you gain/lose 1:1 with the price
FuturesStandardized contract to buy/sell an asset at a future date & agreed priceLinear, leveraged; used for hedging & directional bets
OptionsThe right (not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) at a strike by expiryNon-linear: payoff is max(S−K,0) etc. — the whole reason stochastic calculus exists
ETFA fund (basket of assets) that trades like a single stockCheap, liquid exposure to an index/sector

Equities and futures have linear payoffs — simple to reason about. Options are non-linear: their value depends on volatility and time in ways that require the Year 1 Q2 machinery (Itô, Black-Scholes). That non-linearity is where much of quant pricing lives.

Long, short, and the two prices

To go long is to buy, profiting if the price rises. To go short is to sell something you borrow, profiting if it falls (you buy it back cheaper). At any moment the market shows two prices:

bid = highest price a buyer will pay   |   ask (offer) = lowest price a seller will accept

The gap is the bid-ask spread; the midpoint is the mid price. If you cross the spread to trade immediately, you pay it — this is a real, recurring cost that Lesson 003 and the whole microstructure quarter dissect.

Anatomy of a trade

intention  →  order  →  exchange matching engine  →  fill(s)  →  position  →  P&L
                 │
          market order  (take liquidity now, pay the spread)
          limit order   (post a price, wait, maybe get filled)

A market order demands immediacy and pays the spread; a limit order offers liquidity at your price and may never fill. This single distinction — taking vs making liquidity — drives execution cost, market-making, and much of Year 3 Q2.

Notation you'll reuse

  • S_t — price of the underlying asset at time t
  • K — strike price of an option; T — expiry
  • Call payoff at expiry: max(S_T − K, 0); put: max(K − S_T, 0)
  • Notional — the total face value of a position (not the cash you put up)
Trap. Treating the "price" as a single number. There is no single price — there is a bid, an ask, and a stream of trades. Backtests that assume you buy and sell at the mid (or worse, the close) with no spread or impact are the most common way beginners overstate returns. We fix this properly in the execution quarter, but start distrusting "the price" now.

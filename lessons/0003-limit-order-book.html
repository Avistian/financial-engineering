The Limit Order Book & Price-Time Priority

Lesson 003 · Foundations · ~55 min

Warm up

Modern electronic markets are run by a limit order book (LOB): a live, sorted list of all resting buy and sell orders, maintained by the exchange's matching engine. It is the closest thing the market has to a ground-truth data structure, and it updates thousands of times a second in liquid names. Nearly every microstructure signal you'll build later — order-flow imbalance, queue position, Hawkes-modeled arrivals, DeepLOB-style predictors — is a function of the LOB and how it changes over time.

Understanding this object now pays off twice: it's where the spread and market impact from Lesson 002 come from mechanically, and it's the raw material of the Year 2 Q3 microstructure alpha. Today is about the mechanics; the alpha comes later.

This lesson's single skill: read a limit order book and predict what the matching engine does to an incoming order under price-time (FIFO) priority.

Here the best bid is 100.00 (400 shares want to buy) and the best ask is 100.01 (300 shares offered). The spread is 0.01 and the mid is 100.005. The best bid and ask together are the top of book (Level 1); the full ladder of deeper prices and sizes is Level 2 data. The total quantity resting at a price is that level's depth — a rough gauge of how much you can trade there before pushing the price.

Tick size — why the spread is often exactly one tick

Prices don't move continuously; they move in discrete steps called the tick size (here, 0.01). The tick sets the minimum possible spread: you cannot quote a bid and ask closer than one tick apart. In a heavily traded, liquid name, competition to be at the front of the queue drives the spread down to exactly one tick — the market is "tick-constrained," and the interesting competition moves from price (everyone is at the best price) to queue position and size. In a thin or high-priced name the spread can be many ticks wide. This single parameter quietly shapes strategy: a tick that is large relative to price makes queueing valuable; a tiny relative tick makes price-stepping cheap. Regulators tune tick sizes precisely because they change market behavior.

Price-time priority (FIFO)

The matching engine ranks resting orders by two keys, in order:

  1. Price — better prices match first (higher bids, lower asks).
  2. Time — at the same price, whoever posted earlier is filled first (first-in, first-out).

Why this rule? It is simple, transparent, and it rewards providing liquidity early: if you post a resting order and wait, you keep your place in line. That makes queue position a genuine, tradable edge — two orders at the same price are not equal. The one that arrived earlier gets filled first, but it also faces different adverse selection: front-of-queue orders tend to get filled precisely when the market is about to move against them (an informed trader hits them first). Modeling that trade-off is a whole sub-field. Not every venue uses pure FIFO — some options and futures markets use pro-rata (fills split proportionally to size) or size-priority rules — but price-time is the canonical default, so master it first.

The book is a stream of messages

A static snapshot hides what the book really is: a process. The exchange broadcasts a stream of events that mutate the book — three kinds dominate:

  • Add — a new limit order rests at a price (joins the back of that level's queue).
  • Cancel — a resting order is withdrawn (its queue slot disappears).
  • Trade / execute — an incoming marketable order matches against resting orders.

A fact that surprises newcomers: the vast majority of messages in a modern equity book are adds and cancels, not trades. Liquidity flickers in and out constantly as market makers reprice. This is why "how the book changes" carries more information than any single snapshot, and why reconstructing the book from a message feed (which the Year 2 Q3 labs do) is a core microstructure skill. It's also why naïve signals that read a frozen snapshot get fooled: the size you see may vanish the instant you try to trade against it.

Before you trace the order, commit a prediction:

Walk an order through (a market buy)

Using the book above, a market buy for 500 shares consumes liquidity from the ask side, cheapest first:

1300 @ 100.01ask level 100.01 emptied
2200 @ 100.02100.02 now has 300 left
—average fill ≈ 100.014the order "walked the book"

Compute it: (300 × 100.01 + 200 × 100.02) / 500 = 100.014. The buyer paid more than the best ask (100.01) on average — that gap is slippage (a.k.a. market impact), and it grows with order size relative to available depth.

A second walk (a market sell) — and a resting limit

Now flip it: a market sell for 700 hits the bid side, highest price first: 400 @ 100.00, then 300 @ 99.99.

avg fill = (400 × 100.00 + 300 × 99.99) / 700 = 99.9957

You sold below the best bid (100.00) on average — slippage of about 0.0043, in the direction that hurts a seller. Symmetric to the buy: takers always get the worse side of their own impact.

Contrast a resting limit. Suppose instead you post a limit buy for 500 @ 100.00. You do not cross the spread; you join the 400 already queued at 100.00, sitting behind them (time priority). You might fill 0, some, or all of your 500 depending on how much sells into the bid before you're reached — and if the price runs away upward, you fill nothing and miss the move. That is the maker's bargain: no spread paid, but execution risk and queue uncertainty instead. Deciding when to take (pay the spread, guarantee the fill) versus make (save the spread, risk no fill) is the whole of execution tactics.

Mid price vs micro-price (the book leans)

The mid (100.005) treats both sides equally, but the sizes tell you which way pressure leans. The micro-price weights each side's price by the opposite side's size:

micro = (Q_bid·P_ask + Q_ask·P_bid) / (Q_bid + Q_ask) = (400·100.01 + 300·100.00)/700 ≈ 100.0057

Because there's more size resting on the bid (400) than the ask (300), the micro-price sits above the mid — the book is leaning to buy, and the next tick is a touch more likely to be up. This is a first, tiny taste of extracting a directional signal from the book's shape rather than its level. It won't make you money alone, but it's the conceptual seed of the next section.

Order-flow imbalance — a preview of the signal

The single most durable microstructure signal is order-flow imbalance (OFI): over a short window, is buy pressure (adds to the bid, trades lifting the ask) outrunning sell pressure, or vice versa? Empirically, short-horizon price changes line up with OFI far better than with raw past returns — the book's flow leads its price. You'll build and validate an OFI→return relationship properly in Year 2 Q3 (with correct temporal splits, because it's dangerously easy to leak the future here). For now, just hold the intuition: the book is not a static ledger, it's a pressure gauge, and the change in that pressure is where the edge lives.

Market impact and the square-root law

Slippage isn't just an accounting nuisance — it obeys a remarkably robust empirical regularity. The price impact of executing a large order scales roughly with the square root of its size relative to daily volume (the "square-root law"). Doubling your size does not double your impact; it multiplies it by about √2. This is why splitting a big parent order into many small child orders over time is standard practice, and it's the empirical backbone of the Almgren-Chriss optimal-execution framework (Year 2 Q3). The mechanical fact underneath — that liquidity is finite and consuming it moves the price — is the seed of the entire execution literature.

Trap. Assuming you can trade unlimited size at the best quote (or at the mid). Real fills walk the book and move the price against you, and the size you see on screen can be canceled the instant you reach for it. A backtest that fills large orders at a single quoted price is silently manufacturing returns that don't exist — and the error grows with your assumed position size. Every serious execution model exists because liquidity is finite.

Explain it back

Common questions (and honest answers)

“Why not always post limit orders and never pay the spread?”
Because a limit order is not guaranteed to fill — the market can walk away from your price, and you get nothing. You trade the spread cost for execution risk. Whether to make or take is a real optimization (urgency vs cost), not a free lunch.
“Do I really need Level 2 depth, or is top-of-book enough?”
For low-frequency, small-size strategies, top-of-book is often plenty. But any signal about impact, queue dynamics, or order-flow imbalance lives in the deeper levels and the message stream — you can't measure how a large order will walk the book, or compute OFI, from a single Level-1 number.
“My strategy is tiny — can't I ignore impact?”
Maybe, and that's a legitimate simplifying assumption to state and check, not to assume silently. As soon as size grows relative to depth, impact becomes the dominant cost, and a strategy that ignored it can flip from profitable to losing.
“If the book leans to buy, can't I just trade the micro-price?”
Not naïvely. The lean is real but tiny and fleeting, other people see it too, and the resting size can be spoofed or canceled. It's a genuine seed of signal, but turning it into net-of-cost profit requires the careful, leakage-free treatment of Year 2 Q3 — which is exactly the point of doing this properly.

The Limit Order Book & Price-Time Priority

Lesson 003 · Foundations · ~40 min

Modern electronic markets are run by a limit order book (LOB): a live, sorted list of all resting buy and sell orders. Nearly every microstructure signal you'll build later — order-flow imbalance, queue position, Hawkes-modeled arrivals — is a function of the LOB and how it changes. Understand the object now; the alpha comes in Year 2 Q3.

This lesson's single skill: read a limit order book and predict what a matching engine does to an incoming order under price-time (FIFO) priority.

Price-time priority (FIFO)

The matching engine ranks resting orders by two keys, in order:

  1. Price — better prices match first (higher bids, lower asks).
  2. Time — at the same price, whoever posted earlier is filled first (first-in, first-out).

This is why queue position is a tradable edge: two orders at the same price are not equal — the one that arrived earlier gets filled first and faces different adverse-selection risk.

The buyer paid more than the best ask on average — that gap is slippage / market impact, and it grows with order size relative to available liquidity. This mechanical fact is the seed of the entire impact/execution literature (Almgren-Chriss, the square-root law).

Trap. Assuming you can trade unlimited size at the best quote (or at the mid). Real fills walk the book and move the price against you. A backtest that fills large orders at a single quoted price is silently manufacturing returns that don't exist. Every serious execution model exists because liquidity is finite.
